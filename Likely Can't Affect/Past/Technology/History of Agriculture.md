
1. **Neolithic Revolution (10,000 BCE)**
   - Humans independently domesticate crops in Fertile Crescent and Americas, enabling settled civilization.

2. **Irrigation Systems (6000 BCE)**
   - Mesopotamian farmers develop canals and ditches, controlling water to boost crop yields.

3. **Plows and Draft Animals (4000 BCE)**
   - Ox-drawn plows increase agricultural productivity, allowing larger populations and specialization.

4. **Rice Cultivation (3000 BCE)**
   - Wet rice farming in Asia supports dense populations and complex societies.

5. **Columbian Exchange (1492 onwards)**
   - Plants and animals transfer between hemispheres, transforming agriculture and diet worldwide.

6. **Enclosure Movement (1600s–1800s)**
   - British consolidation of common lands increases efficiency but displaces peasant farmers.

7. **Crop Rotation Refinement (1730s)**
   - Four-field rotation including turnips and clover improves soil fertility.

8. **Cotton Gin (1793)**
   - Eli Whitney's machine revolutionizes cotton processing, tragically intensifying American slavery.

9. **Steel Plow (1837)**
   - John Deere's invention breaks tough prairie soil, opening American Great Plains to farming.

10. **Mechanical Reaper (1831)**
    - Cyrus McCormick's harvester mechanizes grain cutting, reducing labor requirements dramatically.

11. **Chemical Fertilizers (1840s)**
    - Synthetic fertilizers boost yields beyond what organic methods could achieve.

12. **Refrigerated Transport (1870s)**
    - Refrigerated rail cars and ships enable long-distance meat and produce shipment.

13. **Tractors Replace Horses (1900s–1920s)**
    - Gasoline-powered tractors mechanize farming, freeing millions of acres from growing animal feed.

14. **Hybrid Corn (1920s–1930s)**
    - Scientific crossbreeding dramatically increases corn yields across United States.

15. **Combine Harvester (1930s)**
    - Machines that reap, thresh, and clean grain revolutionize harvest efficiency.

16. **Pesticides and Herbicides (1940s)**
    - DDT and chemical weed killers increase yields but create environmental and health concerns.

17. **Green Revolution (1960s–1970s)**
    - Norman Borlaug's high-yield wheat varieties prevent predicted famines in developing world.

18. **Industrial Livestock Operations (1960s)**
    - Concentrated animal feeding operations (CAFOs) industrialize meat production at massive scale.

19. **Organic Farming Movement (1970s)**
    - Growing awareness of pesticide dangers sparks alternative agriculture emphasizing sustainability.

20. **Genetically Modified Crops (1990s)**
    - Bt corn and Roundup Ready soybeans introduce controversial transgenic agriculture.

21. **Precision Agriculture (2000s)**
    - GPS, sensors, and data analytics optimize planting, fertilizing, and harvesting field by field.

22. **Lab-Grown Meat (2013 onwards)**
    - Cultured meat from cells aims to provide protein without raising and slaughtering animals.
