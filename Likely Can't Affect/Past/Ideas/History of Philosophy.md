
1. **Pre-Socratic Philosophy (6th–5th Century BCE)**
   - Early Greek thinkers like Thales, Heraclitus, and Parmenides seeking natural explanations for the cosmos and reality.

2. **Socrates and the Socratic Method (470–399 BCE)**
   - Revolutionary approach using questioning and dialogue to expose ignorance and pursue wisdom and virtue.

3. **Plato and the Theory of Forms (428–348 BCE)**
   - Doctrine of eternal, perfect Forms underlying imperfect material reality, and vision of the ideal state.

4. **Aristotle and Systematic Philosophy (384–322 BCE)**
   - Comprehensive system covering logic, ethics, politics, metaphysics, and natural science grounded in empirical observation.

5. **Hellenistic Philosophy: Stoicism and Epicureanism (3rd Century BCE–3rd Century CE)**
   - Competing schools offering practical wisdom for living well through reason, virtue, and managing desires.

6. **Augustine and Christian Philosophy (354–430 CE)**
   - Synthesis of Platonic philosophy and Christian theology emphasizing grace, free will, and the problem of evil.

7. **Islamic Golden Age Philosophy (8th–13th Century)**
   - Scholars like Al-Farabi, Avicenna, and Averroes preserving Greek thought and developing rational theology and metaphysics.

8. **Thomas Aquinas and Scholasticism (1225–1274)**
   - Integration of Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine through systematic theology and natural law theory.

9. **Renaissance Humanism (14th–16th Century)**
   - Revival of classical learning emphasizing human dignity, individual potential, and secular concerns.

10. **René Descartes and Modern Rationalism (1596–1650)**
    - Methodological doubt establishing "I think, therefore I am" as foundation for certain knowledge and mind-body dualism.

11. **John Locke and Empiricism (1632–1704)**
    - Argument that all knowledge derives from sensory experience and defense of natural rights and limited government.

12. **David Hume and Skeptical Empiricism (1711–1776)**
    - Radical skepticism about causation, induction, and the self, challenging rational foundations of knowledge.

13. **Immanuel Kant and the Critical Philosophy (1724–1804)**
    - Synthesis of rationalism and empiricism arguing that mind actively structures experience and categorical imperative as moral law.

14. **Georg Hegel and German Idealism (1770–1831)**
    - Dialectical method and vision of history as rational Spirit unfolding through thesis, antithesis, and synthesis.

15. **Karl Marx and Historical Materialism (1818–1883)**
    - Critique of capitalism through dialectical materialism viewing history as class struggle driven by economic forces.

16. **Søren Kierkegaard and Existentialism (1813–1855)**
    - Emphasis on individual existence, subjective truth, anxiety, and the leap of faith required for authentic living.

17. **Friedrich Nietzsche and the Death of God (1844–1900)**
    - Proclamation that God is dead, critique of traditional morality, and vision of the Übermensch creating new values.

18. **Pragmatism: Peirce, James, and Dewey (Late 19th–Early 20th Century)**
    - American philosophy judging ideas by their practical consequences and usefulness rather than abstract truth.

19. **Bertrand Russell and Analytic Philosophy (1872–1970)**
    - Logical analysis of language and mathematics as tools for solving philosophical problems through clarity and precision.

20. **Ludwig Wittgenstein and Language Philosophy (1889–1951)**
    - Early focus on logical structure of language, later emphasis on ordinary language use and meaning as use in context.

21. **Martin Heidegger and Being (1889–1976)**
    - Fundamental ontology exploring the meaning of Being through analysis of human existence (Dasein) and temporality.

22. **Jean-Paul Sartre and Existential Freedom (1905–1980)**
    - Atheistic existentialism declaring existence precedes essence and humans are radically free and responsible for creating meaning.

23. **Simone de Beauvoir and Feminist Philosophy (1908–1986)**
    - Analysis of women's oppression as socially constructed and the declaration that "one is not born, but becomes a woman."

24. **Michel Foucault and Power/Knowledge (1926–1984)**
    - Genealogical method revealing how power operates through institutions, discourses, and knowledge to shape subjects.

25. **Jacques Derrida and Deconstruction (1930–2004)**
    - Strategy of reading exposing contradictions and instabilities in texts, challenging binary oppositions and presence.

26. **John Rawls and Political Philosophy (1921–2002)**
    - Theory of justice as fairness using the original position and veil of ignorance to derive principles of a just society.

27. **Martha Nussbaum and the Capabilities Approach (1947–Present)**
    - Framework for justice and human development based on what people are able to do and be rather than resources alone.
