
1. **Mercantilism (16th–18th Century)**
   - Belief that national wealth depended on accumulating precious metals through favorable trade balances and state intervention.

2. **The Physiocrats (1750s–1770s)**
   - French thinkers arguing that agriculture was the only true source of wealth and advocating for laissez-faire economic policy.

3. **Classical Economics — Adam Smith (1776)**
   - The Wealth of Nations establishing free markets, division of labor, and the "invisible hand" as foundations of prosperity.

4. **Comparative Advantage — David Ricardo (1817)**
   - Theory demonstrating how nations benefit from trade by specializing in goods they produce most efficiently.

5. **Marxist Economics — Karl Marx (1867)**
   - Critique of capitalism revealing labor exploitation, surplus value, and prediction of capitalism's inevitable collapse.

6. **Marginalist Revolution (1870s)**
   - Jevons, Menger, and Walras introducing marginal utility theory and mathematical analysis to economic thinking.

7. **Neoclassical Economics (1890s–1930s)**
   - Marshall synthesizing classical and marginalist ideas into framework of supply, demand, and market equilibrium.

8. **Keynesian Revolution — John Maynard Keynes (1936)**
   - The General Theory arguing that aggregate demand drives employment and government intervention stabilizes economies.

9. **The Austrian School — Hayek and von Mises (1940s–1950s)**
   - Defense of free markets, critique of central planning, and emphasis on spontaneous order and knowledge dispersal.

10. **Game Theory — von Neumann and Morgenstern (1944)**
    - Mathematical framework for analyzing strategic interaction and decision-making under uncertainty.

11. **Monetarism — Milton Friedman (1960s–1970s)**
    - Theory emphasizing money supply control over fiscal policy and arguing markets self-correct better than governments.

12. **Rational Expectations Revolution (1970s)**
    - Lucas and others showing how forward-looking behavior limits government policy effectiveness and creates new classical macroeconomics.

13. **New Keynesian Economics (1980s–1990s)**
    - Integration of microeconomic foundations with Keynesian insights about market imperfections and sticky prices.

14. **Behavioral Economics — Kahneman and Tversky (1979–Present)**
    - Systematic study of how psychological factors and cognitive biases cause deviations from rational decision-making.

15. **Complexity Economics (1980s–Present)**
    - Application of complex systems theory viewing economies as adaptive, evolving networks rather than equilibrium systems.
