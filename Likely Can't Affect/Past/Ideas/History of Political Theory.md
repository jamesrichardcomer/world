
1. **Plato's Republic (380 BCE)**
   - Vision of the ideal state ruled by philosopher-kings, with justice defined as each class performing its proper function.

2. **Aristotle's Politics (350 BCE)**
   - Systematic analysis of constitutions and argument that humans are political animals who achieve fulfillment through civic participation.

3. **Cicero and Roman Republicanism (50 BCE)**
   - Defense of mixed government, rule of law, and natural law theory influencing Western constitutional thought.

4. **St. Augustine's City of God (426 CE)**
   - Christian political theology distinguishing earthly and divine authority, justifying secular rule while subordinating it to spiritual ends.

5. **St. Thomas Aquinas' Political Writings (1260s)**
   - Synthesis of Aristotelian philosophy and Christian theology establishing natural law as foundation for legitimate political authority.

6. **Niccolò Machiavelli's The Prince (1532)**
   - Pragmatic analysis of power arguing that effective rulers must be willing to act immorally when necessary for state survival.

7. **Thomas Hobbes' Leviathan (1651)**
   - Social contract theory arguing that rational individuals consent to absolute sovereign authority to escape the state of nature.

8. **John Locke's Two Treatises of Government (1689)**
   - Defense of natural rights, limited government, and the right of revolution against tyrannical rule.

9. **Montesquieu's The Spirit of the Laws (1748)**
   - Analysis of how separation of powers and checks and balances prevent tyranny and preserve liberty.

10. **Jean-Jacques Rousseau's The Social Contract (1762)**
    - Argument that legitimate authority derives from the general will and that true freedom requires self-governance.

11. **The Federalist Papers (1787-1788)**
    - Defense of the U.S. Constitution articulating principles of federalism, representation, and constitutional republicanism.

12. **Edmund Burke's Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790)**
    - Conservative critique of radical change defending tradition, gradual reform, and inherited institutions.

13. **John Stuart Mill's On Liberty (1859)**
    - Liberal defense of individual freedom arguing for minimal state interference except to prevent harm to others.

14. **Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels' The Communist Manifesto (1848)**
    - Revolutionary call for proletarian uprising and abolition of private property to end class exploitation.

15. **John Rawls' A Theory of Justice (1971)**
    - Liberal egalitarian framework using the veil of ignorance to derive principles of justice favoring the least advantaged.

16. **Robert Nozick's Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974)**
    - Libertarian argument that only a minimal state protecting basic rights is morally justified.

17. **Michel Foucault's Power/Knowledge Theories (1970s)**
    - Analysis of how power operates through discourse, institutions, and knowledge production rather than just coercion.

18. **Hannah Arendt's The Origins of Totalitarianism (1951)**
    - Examination of how totalitarian regimes destroy political plurality and human dignity through ideology and terror.
