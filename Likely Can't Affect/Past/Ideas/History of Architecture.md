
1. **Ancient Egyptian Architecture (3000–300 BCE)**
   - Monumental stone structures like pyramids and temples emphasizing permanence, geometry, and alignment with celestial bodies.

2. **Classical Greek Architecture (800–146 BCE)**
   - Development of the three orders (Doric, Ionic, Corinthian) and temples like the Parthenon establishing principles of harmony and proportion.

3. **Roman Architecture (509 BCE–476 CE)**
   - Innovations in concrete, arches, vaults, and domes enabling massive public works like the Colosseum and Pantheon.

4. **Byzantine Architecture (330–1453 CE)**
   - Massive domes, pendentives, and elaborate mosaics exemplified by Hagia Sophia blending Roman engineering with Eastern spirituality.

5. **Romanesque Architecture (800–1200 CE)**
   - Thick walls, round arches, and fortress-like churches reflecting medieval feudalism and religious devotion.

6. **Gothic Architecture (1150–1500 CE)**
   - Pointed arches, flying buttresses, and soaring heights with stained glass creating transcendent sacred spaces.

7. **Renaissance Architecture (1400–1600)**
   - Revival of classical principles with emphasis on symmetry, proportion, and mathematical harmony led by Brunelleschi and Palladio.

8. **Baroque Architecture (1600–1750)**
   - Dramatic curves, ornate decoration, and theatrical spatial effects expressing power of church and monarchy.

9. **Neoclassical Architecture (1750–1850)**
   - Return to pure classical forms emphasizing reason, democracy, and civic virtue in government buildings and institutions.

10. **Industrial Revolution and Iron Architecture (1800s)**
    - Cast iron and steel construction enabling new building types like train stations, markets, and eventually skyscrapers.

11. **Arts and Crafts Movement (1880–1910)**
    - Rejection of industrialization favoring handcrafted details, natural materials, and integration with landscape.

12. **Art Nouveau (1890–1910)**
    - Organic forms, flowing lines, and decorative elements inspired by nature challenging rectilinear tradition.

13. **Modernism and the Bauhaus (1919–1933)**
    - "Form follows function" philosophy, rejection of ornament, and industrial materials creating new architectural language.

14. **International Style (1920s–1970s)**
    - Glass curtain walls, steel frames, and minimalist aesthetics in corporate towers and housing projects worldwide.

15. **Brutalism (1950s–1970s)**
    - Raw concrete, massive geometric forms, and sculptural expressions of structure in civic and institutional buildings.

16. **Postmodernism (1970s–1990s)**
    - Playful rejection of modernist orthodoxy incorporating historical references, color, and ornamentation.

17. **Deconstructivism (1980s–Present)**
    - Fragmented forms, non-rectilinear shapes, and deliberate instability challenging conventional notions of structure and space.

18. **Sustainable and Green Architecture (1990s–Present)**
    - Integration of environmental performance, energy efficiency, and ecological responsibility as core design principles.

19. **Parametric and Digital Architecture (2000s–Present)**
    - Computer-aided design enabling complex curved forms and mass customization previously impossible to build.

20. **Contemporary Adaptive Reuse (2000s–Present)**
    - Transforming historic industrial and commercial structures into new uses preserving cultural heritage while meeting modern needs.
