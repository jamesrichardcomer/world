
1. **Classical Greek and Roman Dress (800 BCE–476 CE)**
   - Draped garments like the chiton and toga that emphasized natural body form and established enduring aesthetic principles.

2. **Medieval European Fashion (500–1400 CE)**
   - Layered tunics, sumptuary laws regulating dress by class, and the emergence of tailoring and fitted garments.

3. **Italian Renaissance Fashion (1400–1600)**
   - Elaborate fabrics, slashed sleeves, and the birth of fashion as artistic expression among wealthy merchant classes.

4. **Baroque and Rococo Fashion (1600–1789)**
   - Extravagant wigs, panniers, ornate decoration, and the French court's dominance in setting European fashion trends.

5. **Victorian Era (1837–1901)**
   - Crinolines, bustles, tight-lacing corsets, and strict dress codes reflecting industrial prosperity and moral conservatism.

6. **Charles Frederick Worth and Haute Couture (1858)**
   - Establishment of the first haute couture house in Paris, creating the designer as artist and seasonal collections model.

7. **The Roaring Twenties and Flapper Fashion (1920s)**
   - Dropped waists, short skirts, bobbed hair, and androgynous silhouettes embodying post-war liberation and modernity.

8. **Christian Dior's New Look (1947)**
   - Revolutionary post-war silhouette with nipped waists and full skirts restoring femininity and luxury to fashion.

9. **Youth Rebellion and Rock 'n' Roll Style (1950s)**
   - Teenage consumer culture emerging with jeans, leather jackets, and distinct youth fashion rejecting parental norms.

10. **The Mini Skirt Revolution (1960s)**
    - Mary Quant's miniskirt, Mod fashion, and youth-driven styles challenging traditional dress codes and sexual mores.

11. **Punk Fashion (1970s)**
    - DIY aesthetic, safety pins, torn clothing, and deliberately shocking styles as political statement and youth rebellion.

12. **Power Dressing (1980s)**
    - Shoulder pads, bold colors, designer labels, and conspicuous consumption reflecting corporate culture and materialism.

13. **Grunge and Anti-Fashion (1990s)**
    - Flannel shirts, ripped jeans, and deliberately unkempt style rejecting glamour and commercialism in favor of authenticity.

14. **Fast Fashion Emergence (1990s–2000s)**
    - Zara, H&M, and rapid production cycles democratizing runway trends but raising sustainability and labor concerns.

15. **Social Media and Influencer Culture (2010s–Present)**
    - Instagram, TikTok, and influencers transforming how fashion is marketed, consumed, and trends are created.
