
1. **The Epic of Gilgamesh (c. 2100 BCE)**
   - Ancient Mesopotamian epic exploring friendship, mortality, and the human condition in one of history's earliest written narratives.

2. **Homer's Iliad and Odyssey (c. 8th Century BCE)**
   - Foundational Greek epics of war and adventure that shaped Western literature and storytelling for millennia.

3. **The Bible (Compiled 2nd Century BCE–2nd Century CE)**
   - Sacred text becoming the most printed and distributed book in history, profoundly influencing Western culture and literacy.

4. **The Divine Comedy — Dante Alighieri (1320)**
   - Italian epic journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise establishing vernacular literature and influencing Western imagination.

5. **Don Quixote — Miguel de Cervantes (1605)**
   - Satirical masterpiece about a delusional knight-errant widely considered the first modern novel and enduring literary achievement.

6. **Robinson Crusoe — Daniel Defoe (1719)**
   - Pioneering adventure novel about survival and self-reliance that helped establish the novel as popular literary form.

7. **Pride and Prejudice — Jane Austen (1813)**
   - Witty social comedy of manners perfecting the novel of character and establishing enduring romance narrative conventions.

8. **Frankenstein — Mary Shelley (1818)**
   - Gothic novel exploring scientific ambition and creation that founded science fiction and continues resonating with modern anxieties.

9. **The Count of Monte Cristo — Alexandre Dumas (1844)**
    - Epic tale of betrayal and revenge that mastered serialized storytelling and captivated mass audiences.

10. **Uncle Tom's Cabin — Harriet Beecher Stowe (1852)**
    - Anti-slavery novel that galvanized abolitionist sentiment and demonstrated literature's power to influence social change.

11. **Moby-Dick — Herman Melville (1851)**
    - Epic whaling adventure exploring obsession, fate, and American identity, initially unsuccessful but now canonical.

12. **Adventures of Huckleberry Finn — Mark Twain (1884)**
    - American classic using vernacular language and child's perspective to explore racism, freedom, and moral development.

13. **The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes — Arthur Conan Doyle (1892)**
    - Detective stories establishing enduring genre conventions and creating literature's most famous investigator.

14. **Dracula — Bram Stoker (1897)**
    - Gothic horror novel creating the definitive vampire mythology that continues dominating popular culture.

15. **The Great Gatsby — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925)**
    - Jazz Age masterpiece critiquing the American Dream through tragic romance and lavish prose.

16. **1984 — George Orwell (1949)**
    - Dystopian warning about totalitarianism, surveillance, and truth manipulation remaining urgently relevant.

17. **The Catcher in the Rye — J.D. Salinger (1951)**
    - Teenage alienation novel capturing adolescent voice and angst, becoming touchstone for generations of readers.

18. **To Kill a Mockingbird — Harper Lee (1960)**
    - Southern novel addressing racial injustice through child's eyes, becoming required reading and cultural landmark.

19. **Harry Potter Series — J.K. Rowling (1997–2007)**
    - Fantasy saga reviving children's literature, creating global phenomenon, and demonstrating books' continued cultural power in digital age.

20. **The Hunger Games Trilogy — Suzanne Collins (2008–2010)**
    - Dystopian young adult series capturing post-9/11 anxieties and establishing YA as major commercial and literary force.

21. **Fifty Shades of Grey — E.L. James (2011)**
    - Erotic romance demonstrating self-publishing's commercial viability and breaking taboos around women's sexual fiction.
