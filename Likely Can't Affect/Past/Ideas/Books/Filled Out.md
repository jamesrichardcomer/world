## Volume 1
**The Great Conversation** — Robert Maynard Hutchins

- An extended essay introducing the set, arguing for the importance of a liberal education and the ongoing dialogue between the great minds of Western history.
    

## Volume 2
**The Great Ideas: A Syntopicon [I. Angel to Love]** — Mortimer Adler (Editor)

- The first half of a massive index and study guide that tracks 102 "Great Ideas" across the entire book set, providing references to where each idea is discussed by different authors.
    

## Volume 3
**The Great Ideas: A Syntopicon [II. Man to World]** — Mortimer Adler (Editor)

- The second half of the study guide, covering ideas from "Man" to "World," and including a bibliography of additional readings.
    

## Volume 4
**Homer** (c. 8th Century B.C.)

- **The Iliad**
    
    - An epic poem set during the final year of the Trojan War, focusing on the rage of Achilles and its devastating consequences for both Greeks and Trojans.
        
- **The Odyssey**
    
    - The epic sequel following the Greek hero Odysseus on his perilous ten-year journey home to Ithaca and his wife Penelope after the fall of Troy.
        

## Volume 5
**Aeschylus** (c. 525–456 B.C.)

- **The Suppliant Maidens**
    
    - The fifty daughters of Danaus seek asylum in Argos to escape forced marriage to their Egyptian cousins.
        
- **The Persians**
    
    - A unique tragedy depicting the defeat of the Persian fleet at the Battle of Salamis from the perspective of the Persian court.
        
- **The Seven Against Thebes**
    
    - The story of the accursed sons of Oedipus, Eteocles and Polynices, who kill each other in a battle for the throne of Thebes.
        
- **Prometheus Bound**
    
    - The Titan Prometheus is chained to a rock and punished by Zeus for stealing fire and giving it to humanity.
        
- **Agamemnon** (Oresteia Part 1)
    
    - King Agamemnon returns victorious from Troy only to be murdered by his wife Clytemnestra and her lover.
        
- **Choephoroe** (The Libation Bearers - Oresteia Part 2)
    
    - Agamemnon’s children, Orestes and Electra, reunite to assassinate their mother Clytemnestra to avenge their father.
        
- **Eumenides** (The Furies - Oresteia Part 3)
    
    - Orestes is tormented by the Furies for his matricide until the goddess Athena establishes a court to try him, replacing blood vengeance with justice.
        

**Sophocles** (c. 495–406 B.C.)

- **Oedipus the King**
    
    - The tragic detective story of a king who unknowingly fulfills a prophecy that he will kill his father and marry his mother.
        
- **Oedipus at Colonus**
    
    - The blind, exiled Oedipus finds sanctuary and death near Athens, bestowing a spiritual blessing on the city that harbored him.
        
- **Antigone**
    
    - Oedipus’s daughter defies King Creon’s edict to leave her brother unburied, choosing the laws of the gods over the laws of the state.
        
- **Ajax**
    
    - The Greek hero goes mad after losing a contest for Achilles’ armor and commits suicide to restore his lost honor.
        
- **Electra**
    
    - A retelling of the revenge of Orestes and Electra against their mother, focusing on Electra’s intense grief and hatred.
        
- **Trachiniae** (The Women of Trachis)
    
    - Heracles’ wife Deianira accidentally kills him with a poisoned robe while attempting to use a love potion to regain his affection.
        
- **Philoctetes**
    
    - A wounded Greek archer, abandoned on an island for years, is sought by Odysseus because a prophecy states Troy cannot fall without his bow.
        

**Euripides** (c. 480–406 B.C.)

- **Rhesus**
    
    - A dramatization of a night raid during the Trojan War involving the Thracian king Rhesus (authorship disputed).
        
- **Medea**
    
    - A sorceress takes horrific revenge on her husband Jason (of the Argonauts) for abandoning her to marry a princess, culminating in the murder of their children.
        
- **Hippolytus**
    
    - Queen Phaedra falls in love with her stepson Hippolytus, leading to tragedy when he rejects her and she frames him for rape.
        
- **Alcestis**
    
    - A devoted queen volunteers to die in her husband’s place but is rescued from Death by Heracles in this tragicomedy.
        
- **Heracleidae**
    
    - The children of Heracles, persecuted by their father’s enemy Eurystheus, seek protection and sanctuary in Athens.
        
- **The Suppliants**
    
    - The mothers of the Seven Against Thebes beg Athens to intervene and help recover their sons’ bodies for proper burial.
        
- **The Trojan Women**
    
    - A harrowing look at the suffering of the enslaved women of Troy (Hecuba, Cassandra, Andromache) after their city is sacked.
        
- **Ion**
    
    - A young temple servant discovers his true parentage as the son of the god Apollo and an Athenian princess.
        
- **Helen**
    
    - An alternate myth in which the "real" Helen never went to Troy but waited in Egypt, while a phantom caused the war.
        
- **Andromache**
    
    - The widow of Hector faces persecution and murder plots as a concubine in the house of Neoptolemus, Achilles' son.
        
- **Electra**
    
    - A realistic, psychological retelling of Orestes and Electra’s matricide, stripping away the heroic aura of the myth.
        
- **The Bacchantes** (The Bacchae)
    
    - King Pentheus is torn apart by frenzied female worshippers (including his own mother) for resisting the worship of the god Dionysus.
        
- **Hecuba**
    
    - The former queen of Troy transforms from a grieving victim into a vengeful force after the murder of her last remaining son.
        
- **Heracles Mad**
    
    - Heracles, driven mad by the goddess Hera, kills his wife and children, and must find a way to live with his guilt after sanity returns.
        
- **The Phoenician Maidens**
    
    - A variation on the story of the Seven Against Thebes, focusing on the conflict between the brothers Eteocles and Polynices.
        
- **Orestes**
    
    - A desperate Orestes, condemned to death for matricide, takes hostages and plots murder to save his own life.
        
- **Iphigenia among the Tauri**
    
    - Iphigenia, having been saved from sacrifice years earlier, serves as a priestess in a foreign land where she unexpectedly encounters her brother Orestes.
        
- **Iphigenia at Aulis**
    
    - The prequel to the Trojan War, depicting Agamemnon’s agonizing decision to sacrifice his daughter Iphigenia to allow the Greek fleet to sail.
        
- **The Cyclops**
    
    - A "satyr play" (a comic burlesque) retelling Odysseus’s encounter with the Cyclops Polyphemus, featuring a chorus of drunken satyrs.
        

**Aristophanes** (c. 445–c. 380 B.C.)

- **The Acharnians**
    
    - An Athenian farmer, tired of the Peloponnesian War, makes a private peace treaty with Sparta and enjoys the benefits while his neighbors suffer.
        
- **The Knights**
    
    - A biting political satire portraying the populist leader Cleon as a manipulative slave controlling a senile master representing the Athenian people.
        
- **The Clouds**
    
    - A comedy mocking Socrates and the Sophists, portraying them as teachers of nonsensical rhetoric who help people cheat their way out of debts.
        
- **The Wasps**
    
    - A satire of the Athenian legal system and the citizens' obsession with serving on juries.
        
- **The Peace**
    
    - A farmer flies a giant dung beetle to heaven to rescue the goddess Peace and end the war between Athens and Sparta.
        
- **The Birds**
    
    - Two Athenians leave the city to found a new utopian kingdom in the clouds ("Cloudcuckooland") in alliance with the birds.
        
- **The Frogs**
    
    - The god Dionysus travels to the underworld to bring back a great playwright to save Athens, judging a contest between Aeschylus and Euripides.
        
- **The Lysistrata**
    
    - The women of Greece, led by Lysistrata, organize a sex strike to force their husbands to negotiate peace and end the war.
        
- **The Thesmophoriazusae**
    
    - The women of Athens plot to punish the playwright Euripides for his unflattering portrayals of them; he sends a relative in drag to infiltrate their meeting.
        
- **The Ecclesiazusae** (The Assemblywomen)
    
    - Women take over the Athenian assembly disguised as men and institute a communist state where wealth and lovers are shared.
        
- **The Plutus**
    
    - The god of wealth regains his sight and decides to distribute riches to the virtuous rather than the wicked, upending society.

## Volume 6
**Herodotus** (c. 484–c. 425 B.C.)

- **The History**
    
    - Often called the "Father of History," Herodotus chronicles the rise of the Persian Empire and its conflicts with the Greek city-states (Greco-Persian Wars), while exploring the diverse cultures, geographies, and customs of the known world.
        

**Thucydides** (c. 460–c. 400 B.C.)

- **The History of the Peloponnesian War**
    
    - A strictly analytical and chronological account of the devastating war between Athens and Sparta, focusing on political realism, the psychology of power, and the failure of democracy under stress (e.g., the Melian Dialogue).
        

## Volume 7
**Plato** (c. 428–c. 348 B.C.)

- **Charmides**
    
    - Socrates discusses the definition of _sophrosyne_ (temperance or moderation) with a promising youth who later becomes a tyrant.
        
- **Lysis**
    
    - A dialogue investigating the nature of friendship and desire, ending in aporia (confusion) without a final definition.
        
- **Laches**
    
    - Two generals discuss the definition of courage, which Socrates dismantles, showing it is not merely endurance or knowledge of hope/fear.
        
- **Protagoras**
    
    - Socrates debates the famous Sophist Protagoras on whether virtue can be taught and the unity of the virtues.
        
- **Euthydemus**
    
    - A satire on the logical fallacies and verbal trickery (eristic) employed by Sophists compared to Socratic dialectic.
        
- **Cratylus**
    
    - An inquiry into the correctness of names and whether language is conventional (arbitrary) or natural (inherently descriptive).
        
- **Phaedrus**
    
    - A dialogue on the nature of love and the art of rhetoric, using the allegory of the soul as a charioteer with two winged horses.
        
- **Ion**
    
    - Socrates argues that poets and rhapsodes (performers) work through divine madness and inspiration rather than rational art or skill.
        
- **Symposium**
    
    - A series of speeches on the nature of Eros (Love) given at a drinking party, culminating in Socrates’ account of the ladder of love ascending to the Form of Beauty.
        
- **Meno**
    
    - Explores the definition of virtue and introduces the theory of recollection (learning as remembering eternal truths) through a geometry demonstration with a slave boy.
        
- **Euthyphro**
    
    - Socrates questions a priest about the definition of piety—is something pious because the gods love it, or do the gods love it because it is pious?
        
- **Apology**
    
    - Socrates' defiant speech at his trial, defending his philosophical mission and asserting that "the unexamined life is not worth living."
        
- **Crito**
    
    - Socrates refuses to escape from prison, arguing that a citizen has a social contract to obey the laws of the state, even when unjustly condemned.
        
- **Phaedo**
    
    - Set on the day of Socrates’ execution, this dialogue argues for the immortality of the soul and the existence of the Forms.
        
- **Gorgias**
    
    - An examination of rhetoric vs. philosophy, arguing that it is better to suffer wrong than to do wrong.
        
- **The Republic**
    
    - Plato’s magnum opus on the nature of justice, constructing an ideal city-state ruled by philosopher-kings and introducing the Allegory of the Cave.
        
- **Timaeus**
    
    - A cosmological monologue describing the creation of the universe by a Demiurge using the Forms as a blueprint.
        
- **Critias**
    
    - The unfinished sequel to the _Timaeus_, telling the story of the ancient conflict between Athens and the legendary lost island of Atlantis.
        
- **Parmenides**
    
    - A challenging dialogue where the young Socrates defends his Theory of Forms against devastating criticisms from the elder philosopher Parmenides.
        
- **Theaetetus**
    
    - An investigation into the nature of knowledge, rejecting definitions such as "perception" or "true belief with an account."
        
- **Sophist**
    
    - A sequel to the _Theaetetus_ that defines the Sophist and tackles the problem of "non-being" to explain how falsehood is possible.
        
- **Statesman**
    
    - Defines the true statesman as one possessing the "royal art" of weaving together different temperaments in the city.
        
- **Philebus**
    
    - A debate on whether the good life consists of pleasure or wisdom, concluding that a mixture dominated by intelligence is best.
        
- **Laws**
    
    - Plato’s longest and last work, outlining a detailed (and more practical/theocratic) legal code for a hypothetical second-best city, Magnesia.
        
- **The Seventh Letter**
    
    - An autobiographical letter (generally accepted as authentic) detailing Plato’s failed attempts to educate the tyrant of Syracuse in philosophy.
        

## Volume 8
**Aristotle** (384–322 B.C.)

- **Categories**
    
    - A classification of the different ways things can be (substance, quantity, quality, relation, etc.) and how we predicate terms of them.
        
- **On Interpretation**
    
    - Analysis of propositions, truth, falsity, and the relationship between language and thought.
        
- **Prior Analytics**
    
    - The formal introduction of the syllogism, establishing the rules for valid deductive reasoning.
        
- **Posterior Analytics**
    
    - The theory of scientific demonstration, explaining how knowledge is derived from first principles.
        
- **Topics**
    
    - A handbook for dialectical reasoning and argumentation concerning probable (rather than certain) opinions.
        
- **On Sophistical Refutations**
    
    - A guide to identifying and solving logical fallacies and deceptive arguments.
        
- **Physics**
    
    - An inquiry into the principles of nature, covering matter, form, motion, causation (the Four Causes), time, and the Prime Mover.
        
- **On the Heavens**
    
    - Aristotle’s cosmology, describing the earth as the center of the universe surrounded by concentric, rotating spheres of aether.
        
- **On Generation and Corruption**
    
    - Explains how the four elements (earth, air, fire, water) transform into one another and how substances come into being and pass away.
        
- **Meteorology**
    
    - Studies "sublunary" phenomena like weather, comets, earthquakes, and minerals.
        
- **Metaphysics**
    
    - The study of "being qua being," examining substance, essence, potentiality/actuality, and the ultimate divine substance (God).
        
- **On the Soul** (De Anima)
    
    - An investigation into the nature of the soul (psyche) as the form of the body, covering nutrition, perception, and intellect.
        
- **Parva Naturalia** (Short Treatises)
    
    - A collection of shorter works applying psychology to the body, including _On Sense_, _On Memory_, _On Sleep_, _On Dreams_, _On Longevity_, _On Youth/Old Age_, and _On Breathing_.
        

## Volume 9
**Aristotle** (Continued)

- **History of Animals**
    
    - A massive collection of observational data on the anatomy, behavior, and habitats of various animals.
        
- **On the Parts of Animals**
    
    - Explains the "why" of animal anatomy, arguing that every part serves a specific function (teleology).
        
- **On the Motion of Animals**
    
    - Analyzes the general principles of animal movement using mechanics.
        
- **On the Gait of Animals**
    
    - Studies the specific ways different animals walk, fly, or swim.
        
- **On the Generation of Animals**
    
    - A detailed study of reproduction and embryology.
        
- **Nicomachean Ethics**
    
    - Aristotle’s primary work on how to live well, defining happiness (_eudaimonia_) as virtuous activity in accordance with reason.
        
- **Politics**
    
    - Analyzes the city-state (_polis_) as the natural community for man, classifying different constitutions (monarchy, aristocracy, democracy) and their corrupted forms.
        
- **The Athenian Constitution**
    
    - A historical account of the development of Athens' government (found in the 19th century, added to later editions of the Great Books).
        
- **Rhetoric**
    
    - The art of persuasion, analyzing the character of the speaker (_ethos_), the emotion of the audience (_pathos_), and the argument itself (_logos_).
        
- **On Poetics**
    
    - The first treatise on literary theory, defining tragedy as an imitation of action that produces a catharsis of pity and fear.
        

## Volume 10
**Hippocrates** (fl. 400 B.C.)

- **The Oath**
    
    - The foundational ethical code for physicians, promising to benefit the sick and do no harm.
        
- **On Ancient Medicine**
    
    - A defense of empirical medicine against those who try to base it on philosophical postulates (hot, cold, dry, wet).
        
- **On Airs, Waters, and Places**
    
    - The first work on environmental health, discussing how climate and geography affect disease and culture.
        
- **The Book of Prognostics**
    
    - Teaches physicians how to predict the course of a disease based on observable symptoms.
        
- **On Regimen in Acute Diseases**
    
    - Guidelines for diet and care during severe illnesses like fevers.
        
- **Of the Epidemics**
    
    - Case studies and notes on disease outbreaks observed by the physician.
        
- **On Injuries of the Head**
    
    - Detailed surgical instructions for treating skull fractures and head wounds.
        
- **On the Surgery**
    
    - General advice on the physician’s conduct, bandages, and operating room procedures.
        
- **On Fractures** & **On the Articulations** & **Instruments of Reduction**
    
    - Comprehensive treatises on setting broken bones, treating dislocations, and the orthopedic tools used.
        
- **Aphorisms**
    
    - A famous collection of medical maxims (e.g., "Life is short, art is long").
        
- **The Law**
    
    - A short statement on the education and character required of a physician.
        
- **On Ulcers, Fistulae, Hemorrhoids**
    
    - Specific treatments for these ailments.
        
- **On the Sacred Disease**
    
    - A groundbreaking work arguing that epilepsy is a natural disease with physical causes, not a divine possession.
        

**Galen** (c. A.D. 130–c. 200)

- **On the Natural Faculties**
    
    - A physiological treatise arguing that the body has innate powers (attraction, retention, alteration, expulsion) to process food and maintain life, opposing purely mechanical explanations.

# The Great Books of the Western World: Volumes 11–15
## Volume 11
**Euclid** (fl. c. 300 B.C.)

- **The Thirteen Books of Euclid's Elements**
    
    - The most influential textbook in history, establishing the axiomatic method of geometry. It systematically derives theorems about plane geometry, number theory, and solid geometry from a small set of definitions, postulates, and common notions.
        

**Archimedes** (c. 287–212 B.C.)

- **On the Sphere and Cylinder**
    
    - Proves the surface area and volume of a sphere relative to its circumscribing cylinder.
        
- **Measurement of a Circle**
    
    - Approximates the value of Pi (π) and determines the area of a circle.
        
- **On Conoids and Spheroids**
    
    - Investigates the volumes of solids formed by rotating conic sections (parabolas, hyperbolas, ellipses).
        
- **On Spirals**
    
    - A study of the properties of the "Archimedean spiral."
        
- **On the Equilibrium of Planes**
    
    - Establishes the laws of the lever and the center of gravity for plane figures.
        
- **The Sand-Reckoner**
    
    - A work attempting to calculate the number of grains of sand that would fit in the universe, introducing a system for expressing incredibly large numbers.
        
- **Quadrature of the Parabola**
    
    - Uses the "method of exhaustion" to prove the area of a parabolic segment.
        
- **On Floating Bodies**
    
    - The foundational text of hydrostatics, establishing Archimedes' principle of buoyancy.
        
- **Book of Lemmas**
    
    - A collection of fifteen propositions on circles and geometry.
        
- **The Method Treating of Mechanical Problems**
    
    - Reveals Archimedes' use of mechanical concepts (like leverage) to discover geometric theorems before proving them rigorously.
        

**Apollonius of Perga** (c. 262–c. 200 B.C.)

- **On Conic Sections**
    
    - A comprehensive study of the curves generated by slicing a cone: the ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola. It introduced the terms for these curves still used today.
        

**Nicomachus of Gerasa** (fl. c. A.D. 100)

- **Introduction to Arithmetic**
    
    - A key text on Pythagorean number theory, dealing with the mystical and philosophical properties of numbers rather than practical calculation.
        

## Volume 12
**Lucretius** (c. 98–c. 55 B.C.)

- **On the Nature of Things** (De Rerum Natura)
    
    - A didactic poem expounding the philosophy of Epicurus. It argues that the universe is made of atoms and void, denies the intervention of gods in human affairs, and seeks to eliminate the fear of death by explaining natural phenomena through physical laws.
        

**Epictetus** (c. A.D. 60–c. 138)

- **The Discourses**
    
    - A series of lectures by the former slave and Stoic philosopher. He teaches that we should only concern ourselves with what is in our control (our own will and judgments) and accept what is outside our control with equanimity.
        

**Marcus Aurelius** (A.D. 121–180)

- **The Meditations**
    
    - The private journals of the Roman Emperor. Written on military campaign, they are personal spiritual exercises in Stoic philosophy, reminding himself to remain humble, virtuous, and rational amidst the chaos of empire.
        

## Volume 13
**Virgil** (70–19 B.C.)

- **The Eclogues**
    
    - A collection of ten pastoral poems depicting an idealized rural life of shepherds and their loves, modeled on the Greek poet Theocritus.
        
- **The Georgics**
    
    - A didactic poem on agriculture, covering crops, trees, livestock, and bees. It celebrates the labor of the farmer and the beauty of the Italian landscape while reflecting on the order of nature.
        
- **The Aeneid**
    
    - The national epic of Rome, telling the story of the Trojan hero Aeneas who escapes the fall of Troy and travels to Italy to become the ancestor of the Romans. It celebrates the destiny of Rome under Augustus while acknowledging the tragic cost of empire.
        

## Volume 14
**Plutarch** (c. A.D. 46–c. 120)

- **The Lives of the Noble Grecians and Romans**
    
    - A series of biographies arranged in pairs (one Greek, one Roman) to illuminate moral character and political virtue. Plutarch focuses less on strict history and more on the personal traits and ethical choices of famous figures like Alexander the Great, Julius Caesar, Pericles, and Cicero.
        

## Volume 15
**P. Cornelius Tacitus** (c. A.D. 55–c. 117)

- **The Annals**
    
    - A pessimistic and penetrating history of the Roman Empire under the Julio-Claudian dynasty (Tiberius, Claudius, Nero). Tacitus exposes the corruption of absolute power and the decline of Roman liberty.
        
- **The Histories**
    
    - Covers the chaotic "Year of the Four Emperors" following Nero's death and the rise of the Flavian dynasty. It vividly depicts civil war and the political instability of the era.

## Volume 16
**Ptolemy** (c. A.D. 100–c. 178)

- **The Almagest**
    
    - The definitive text on ancient astronomy. It presents a complex mathematical model of the universe with the Earth at the center (geocentric), using cycles and epicycles to predict the positions of the planets. It remained the authority for over a thousand years.
        

**Nicolaus Copernicus** (1473–1543)

- **On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres**
    
    - The groundbreaking work that proposed the heliocentric model, placing the Sun at the center of the universe. It challenged the Ptolemaic system and laid the foundation for modern astronomy.
        

**Johannes Kepler** (1571–1630)

- **Epitome of Copernican Astronomy [Books IV–V]**
    
    - A systematic defense and explanation of the Copernican system, refined by Kepler's discovery that planetary orbits are elliptical rather than circular.
        
- **The Harmonies of the World [Book V]**
    
    - Explores the mathematical ratios and musical harmonies Kepler believed governed planetary motion. It contains his famous "Third Law" connecting a planet's orbital period to its distance from the Sun.
        

## Volume 17
**Plotinus** (205–270)

- **The Six Enneads**
    
    - The foundational text of Neoplatonism. Plotinus describes a hierarchical universe emanating from "The One" (the transcendent source of all being) down to the Divine Intellect (Nous), the World Soul, and finally matter. The goal of philosophy is the soul's ascent back to union with The One.
        

## Volume 18
**Saint Augustine** (354–430)

- **The Confessions**
    
    - The first true Western autobiography. Augustine traces his spiritual journey from a sinful youth and intellectual wandering (through Manichaeism and Neoplatonism) to his dramatic conversion to Christianity, exploring the nature of memory and time along the way.
        
- **The City of God**
    
    - A monumental philosophy of history written to defend Christianity against the claim that it caused the fall of Rome. Augustine contrasts the "City of God" (those living for God) with the "City of Man" (those living for self), tracing their conflict through history to the end of time.
        
- **On Christian Doctrine**
    
    - A guide to interpreting and teaching the Scriptures. It discusses the relationship between secular learning (rhetoric, philosophy) and Christian theology, arguing that Christians should "plunder the Egyptians" by using useful pagan knowledge for the faith.
        

## Volume 19
**Saint Thomas Aquinas** (c. 1225–1274)

- **Summa Theologica (First Part)**
    
    - **Treatise on God:** Analyzes the existence of God (The Five Ways), His simplicity, perfection, goodness, infinity, and knowledge.
        
    - **Treatise on the Trinity:** Examines the divine persons (Father, Son, Holy Spirit) and their relations.
        
    - **Treatise on the Creation:** Discusses the production of creatures from nothing and the distinction between the Creator and the created.
        

## Volume 20
**Saint Thomas Aquinas** (Continued)

- **Summa Theologica (First Part continued & Second Part)**
    
    - **Treatise on the Angels:** A detailed study of angelic nature, hierarchy, and intellect.
        
    - **Treatise on the Work of the Six Days:** Analysis of the Genesis creation narrative.
        
    - **Treatise on Man:** Investigates the union of body and soul, and the powers of the human soul (intellect, will).
        
    - **Treatise on the Divine Government:** How God rules and sustains the universe.
        
    - **Treatise on the Last End:** Argues that man's ultimate happiness lies in the vision of God.
        
    - **Treatise on Human Acts:** The psychology of voluntary action, will, and intention.
        
    - **Treatise on Habits:** The nature of virtues and vices as ingrained habits.
        
    - **Treatise on Law:** Defines law (Eternal, Natural, Human, Divine) and its role in guiding human acts.
        
    - **Treatise on Grace:** The necessity of divine help for salvation.
        
    - **Treatise on Faith, Hope, and Charity:** The three "theological virtues" that unite man to God.
        
    - **Treatise on Active and Contemplative Life:** Compares the life of action/service with the life of prayer/study.
        
    - **Treatise on the States of Life:** Discusses religious orders and the episcopacy.
        
    - **Treatise on the Incarnation:** How God became man in Jesus Christ.
        
    - **Treatise on the Sacraments:** The physical signs that convey grace (Baptism, Eucharist, etc.).
        
    - **Treatise on the Resurrection & The Last Things:** Eschatology concerning the end of the world and the afterlife.

## Volume 21
**Dante Alighieri** (1265–1321)

- **The Divine Comedy**
    
    - An epic poem describing Dante’s spiritual journey through the three realms of the afterlife.
        
        - **Inferno:** Led by the Roman poet Virgil, Dante descends into Hell, witnessing the punishments of sinners arranged by the severity of their vices.
            
        - **Purgatorio:** Dante climbs the Mountain of Purgatory, where penitent souls are purified of their sins (the Seven Deadly Sins) before entering Heaven.
            
        - **Paradiso:** Guided by his beloved Beatrice, Dante ascends through the celestial spheres of Heaven, culminating in a beatific vision of God.
            

## Volume 22
**Geoffrey Chaucer** (c. 1340–1400)

- **Troilus and Cressida**
    
    - A tragic romance set during the Siege of Troy. It tells the story of the love between the Trojan prince Troilus and the noblewoman Cressida, her eventual betrayal of him for the Greek warrior Diomedes, and the philosophical implications of fortune and free will.
        
- **The Canterbury Tales**
    
    - A collection of stories told by a diverse group of pilgrims (including a Knight, Miller, Wife of Bath, Pardoner, etc.) traveling to the shrine of Thomas Becket. The tales range from bawdy farces to pious sermons, offering a vivid cross-section of medieval English society.
        

## Volume 23
**Nicolò Machiavelli** (1469–1527)

- **The Prince**
    
    - A revolutionary treatise on political power. Machiavelli separates politics from ethics, advising rulers that it is often necessary to use cruelty, deception, and force ("the lion and the fox") to maintain the state and stability, famously arguing it is better to be feared than loved.
        

**Thomas Hobbes** (1588–1679)

- **Leviathan**
    
    - A foundational text of social contract theory. Hobbes argues that the natural state of man is a chaotic "war of all against all." To escape this, individuals surrender their rights to an absolute sovereign (the "Leviathan") who guarantees peace and security through undivided power.
        

## Volume 24
**François Rabelais** (c. 1495–1553)

- **Gargantua and Pantagruel**
    
    - A series of five satirical novels following the absurd and grotesque adventures of two giants, Gargantua and his son Pantagruel. Written in an exuberant, bawdy style, it satirizes religion, education, and politics while celebrating the humanist ideals of the Renaissance (e.g., the Abbey of Thélème's rule: "Do what thou wilt").
        

## Volume 25
**Michel Eyquem de Montaigne** (1533–1592)

- **Essays**
    
    - Montaigne invented the "essay" (from the French _essayer_, "to try") as a literary form to explore his own mind. Rather than systematic philosophy, these are personal, skeptical, and meandering reflections on human nature.
        
    - **Key Themes & Notable Essays:**
        
        - **Skepticism:** Montaigne frequently questions the certainty of human knowledge ("What do I know?"), most famously in the long _Apology for Raimond de Sebonde_.
            
        - **The Self:** He uses himself as the subject of his book, analyzing his own habits, kidney stones, and moods to understand humanity universally.
            
        - **Of Cannibals:** A pioneering work of cultural relativism, comparing the "barbarism" of New World natives favorably against the cruelty of European religious wars.
            
        - **Of Friendship:** A touching tribute to his deceased friend Étienne de La Boétie.
            
        - **Of Experience:** The final essay, celebrating the diversity of life and the wisdom of accepting one's human condition, including the body and its limits.

## Volume 26
**William Shakespeare** (1564–1616)

- **The First Part of King Henry the Sixth**
    
    - Depicts the early loss of France and the origins of the Wars of the Roses between the houses of York and Lancaster.
        
- **The Second Part of King Henry the Sixth**
    
    - Focuses on the inability of the weak King Henry to control his nobles, leading to the rise of Richard, Duke of York.
        
- **The Third Part of King Henry the Sixth**
    
    - The bloody conclusion of the civil war, featuring the brutal rise of the future Richard III.
        
- **The Tragedy of King Richard the Third**
    
    - The hunchbacked villain Richard murders his way to the throne, only to be defeated by the Earl of Richmond (Henry VII).
        
- **The Comedy of Errors**
    
    - A farce involving two sets of identical twins separated at birth, leading to mistaken identities and wrongful beatings.
        
- **Titus Andronicus**
    
    - A gruesome revenge tragedy filled with mutilation, rape, and cannibalism in ancient Rome.
        
- **The Taming of the Shrew**
    
    - Petruchio "tames" the headstrong Katherina into an obedient wife through psychological manipulation and starvation.
        
- **The Two Gentlemen of Verona**
    
    - A comedy about friendship and infidelity, where Proteus betrays his best friend Valentine for the love of Silvia.
        
- **Love’s Labour’s Lost**
    
    - The King of Navarre and his lords swear off women to study for three years, but immediately fall in love with the Princess of France and her ladies.
        
- **Romeo and Juliet**
    
    - The archetypal tragedy of "star-crossed lovers" from feuding families who kill themselves for love.
        
- **The Tragedy of King Richard the Second**
    
    - A poetic history play about the deposition of the incompetent but divinely ordained King Richard II by Henry Bolingbroke.
        
- **A Midsummer-Night’s Dream**
    
    - A magical comedy involving four lovers, a group of amateur actors, and the fairy king and queen in an enchanted forest.
        
- **The Life and Death of King John**
    
    - Deals with the question of legitimacy and the political machinations involving France and the Pope; the Magna Carta era (though not the signing itself).
        
- **The Merchant of Venice**
    
    - A Venetian merchant defaults on a loan to the Jewish moneylender Shylock, who demands a "pound of flesh" as payment.
        
- **The First Part of King Henry the Fourth**
    
    - King Henry IV faces rebellion while his son, Prince Hal, wastes time in taverns with the roguish Sir John Falstaff.
        
- **The Second Part of King Henry the Fourth**
    
    - Prince Hal eventually accepts his royal responsibilities and rejects Falstaff upon becoming King Henry V.
        
- **Much Ado About Nothing**
    
    - A comedy featuring the "merry war" of wit between Beatrice and Benedick, who are tricked into falling in love.
        
- **The Life of King Henry the Fifth**
    
    - King Henry V unites England and invades France, achieving a miraculous victory at the Battle of Agincourt.
        
- **Julius Caesar**
    
    - Brutus and Cassius assassinate Caesar to save the Republic, but their actions plunge Rome into civil war.
        
- **As You Like It**
    
    - Rosalind flees persecution to the Forest of Arden disguised as a boy, where she instructs her love Orlando on how to woo her.
        

## Volume 27
**William Shakespeare** (Continued)

- **Twelfth Night; or, What You Will**
    
    - Viola, shipwrecked and disguised as a man, falls in love with the Duke she serves, while the Countess he loves falls for Viola.
        
- **Hamlet, Prince of Denmark**
    
    - Prince Hamlet is visited by his father's ghost, who demands revenge against Hamlet's uncle Claudius for murder; Hamlet struggles with existential doubt and delay.
        
- **The Merry Wives of Windsor**
    
    - A farce in which Falstaff attempts to seduce two married women to get their money, but is repeatedly humiliated by them.
        
- **Troilus and Cressida**
    
    - A cynical take on the Trojan War, focusing on the betrayal of Troilus by Cressida and the political gridlock of the Greek camp.
        
- **All’s Well That Ends Well**
    
    - Helena cures the King of France to win the hand of the nobleman Bertram, who flees to war to avoid the marriage.
        
- **Measure for Measure**
    
    - A dark comedy about justice and hypocrisy; the strict judge Angelo sentences a man to death for fornication while trying to blackmail the man's sister into sleeping with him.
        
- **Othello, the Moor of Venice**
    
    - The villain Iago manipulates the Moorish general Othello into believing his faithful wife Desdemona has been unfaithful, driving him to murder her.
        
- **King Lear**
    
    - An aging king divides his kingdom among his daughters based on flattery, leading to his rejection, madness, and tragic death on a stormy heath.
        
- **Macbeth**
    
    - Prompted by witches' prophecies and his wife's ambition, a Scottish general murders his king to take the throne, plunging into paranoia and tyranny.
        
- **Antony and Cleopatra**
    
    - The story of the passionate, doomed romance between the Roman general Mark Antony and the Egyptian queen Cleopatra, which costs them an empire.
        
- **Coriolanus**
    
    - A brilliant Roman general is banished for his arrogance and contempt for the common people; he joins Rome's enemies to lead an army against his own city.
        
- **Timon of Athens**
    
    - A wealthy Athenian gives away his fortune to friends; when they abandon him in his poverty, he becomes a bitter misanthrope living in a cave.
        
- **Pericles, Prince of Tyre**
    
    - A romance following Pericles’ wanderings, the loss of his wife and daughter at sea, and their eventual miraculous reunion.
        
- **Cymbeline**
    
    - A complex romance set in ancient Britain involving a wager on a wife's fidelity, disguised identities, and conflict with Rome.
        
- **The Winter’s Tale**
    
    - King Leontes wrongly accuses his wife of adultery and loses his family, only to be reunited with them years later through a "statue" coming to life.
        
- **The Tempest**
    
    - The sorcerer Prospero, exiled on an island with his daughter, uses magic to shipwreck his enemies and orchestrate a resolution before giving up his power.
        
- **The Famous History of the Life of King Henry the Eighth**
    
    - Depicts the fall of Cardinal Wolsey and Queen Katherine, and the rise of Anne Boleyn and the birth of Elizabeth I.
        
- **Sonnets**
    
    - A sequence of 154 poems exploring themes of time, love, beauty, and infidelity, addressed largely to a "Fair Youth" and a "Dark Lady."
        

## Volume 28
**William Gilbert** (1540–1603)

- **On the Loadstone and Magnetic Bodies**
    
    - The first major scientific work on magnetism. Gilbert proposes that the Earth itself is a giant magnet, explaining why compasses point north.
        

**Galileo Galilei** (1564–1642)

- **Dialogues Concerning the Two New Sciences**
    
    - Galileo’s final work, laying the foundations of modern physics. It covers the strength of materials (engineering) and the motion of objects (kinematics), proving that all falling bodies accelerate at the same rate regardless of weight.
        

**William Harvey** (1578–1657)

- **On the Motion of the Heart and Blood in Animals**
    
    - Revolutionized medicine by proving that the heart acts as a pump to circulate blood in a closed system throughout the body.
        
- **On the Circulation of the Blood**
    
    - Essays defending his circulation theory against critics.
        
- **On the Generation of Animals**
    
    - A study of embryology, famously proposing that all life comes from an egg (_omne vivum ex ovo_) and challenging the idea of spontaneous generation.
        

## Volume 29
**Miguel de Cervantes** (1547–1616)

- **The History of Don Quixote de la Mancha**
    
    - Often cited as the first modern novel. An aging minor noble reads so many romances of chivalry that he loses his mind and sets out as a knight-errant to right wrongs. Accompanied by his realistic, proverb-quoting squire Sancho Panza, Quixote tilts at windmills he believes to be giants. The book is a profound exploration of reality vs. illusion, idealism, and sanity.
        

## Volume 30
**Sir Francis Bacon** (1561–1626)

- **Advancement of Learning**
    
    - A survey of the state of knowledge in Bacon's time, identifying gaps and arguing for the value of learning for practical benefit rather than just contemplation.
        
- **Novum Organum** (The New Organon)
    
    - Outlines Bacon's new scientific method based on inductive reasoning and observation. He famously categorizes the "Idols of the Mind" (Idols of the Tribe, Cave, Marketplace, and Theater)—biases that prevent clear thinking.
        
- **New Atlantis**
    
    - A utopian novel describing the legendary island of Bensalem, which is centered around "Solomon’s House," a state-sponsored research institution dedicated to the study of nature for the betterment of humanity.

## Volume 31
**René Descartes** (1596–1650)

- **Rules for the Direction of the Mind**
    
    - An early, unfinished work outlining rules for clear thinking and scientific problem-solving, emphasizing the reduction of complex problems into simple parts.
        
- **Discourse on the Method**
    
    - Descartes’ manifesto for a new scientific method based on skepticism. It contains his famous foundational maxim, _Cogito, ergo sum_ ("I think, therefore I am").
        
- **Meditations on First Philosophy**
    
    - A deep dive into metaphysics. Descartes employs "methodological doubt" to strip away all uncertain beliefs, then reconstructs knowledge by proving the existence of the self, God, and the external world, establishing the mind-body distinction (Dualism).
        
- **Objections Against the Meditations and Replies**
    
    - Descartes defends his theories against contemporary critics (including Hobbes), clarifying his views on the nature of God and the soul.
        
- **The Geometry**
    
    - A groundbreaking mathematical work that introduced "Cartesian geometry," bridging the gap between algebra and geometry by using equations to describe curves.
        

**Benedict de Spinoza** (1632–1677)

- **Ethics**
    
    - A philosophical system written in the style of a geometry textbook (with definitions, axioms, and propositions). Spinoza argues for a radical monism where "God" and "Nature" are the same substance (_Deus sive Natura_). He contends that freedom comes not from free will (which he denies), but from understanding the rational necessity of the universe.
        

## Volume 32
**John Milton** (1608–1674)

- **English Minor Poems**
    
    - Includes classics like _L’Allegro_ (the cheerful man), _Il Penseroso_ (the contemplative man), and _Lycidas_ (a pastoral elegy on the death of a friend).
        
- **Psalms & Sonnets**
    
    - Milton's translations and personal poems, including his famous sonnet on his blindness ("When I consider how my light is spent").
        
- **Paradise Lost**
    
    - The greatest epic poem in the English language. It retells the biblical story of the Fall of Man: the temptation of Adam and Eve by the fallen angel Satan (depicted as a complex, tragic anti-hero) and their expulsion from the Garden of Eden.
        
- **Samson Agonistes**
    
    - A tragic "closet drama" (meant to be read, not acted) modeled on Greek tragedy. It depicts the final day of the biblical judge Samson, blind and enslaved, as he regains his spiritual strength to destroy his Philistine captors.
        
- **Areopagitica**
    
    - A passionate prose defense of free speech and the liberty of the press, arguing against government censorship and licensing of books.
        

## Volume 33
**Blaise Pascal** (1623–1662)

- **The Provincial Letters**
    
    - A series of witty, satirical letters defending the Jansenist theological movement and attacking the moral laxity (casuistry) of the Jesuits.
        
- **Pensées** (Thoughts)
    
    - A collection of fragments intended for a defense of Christianity (Apology). Pascal explores the "misery and grandeur" of the human condition, arguing that reason alone is insufficient for faith. It includes "Pascal’s Wager," which argues that believing in God is the most rational bet.
        
- **Scientific Treatises**
    
    - **On the Vacuum:** Describes experiments proving the existence of a vacuum (disproving the old Aristotelian idea that "nature abhors a vacuum").
        
    - **On the Equilibrium of Liquids:** Foundational work on hydrostatics and atmospheric pressure.
        
    - **On the Arithmetical Triangle:** Explores the properties of "Pascal's Triangle" and early probability theory.
        

## Volume 34
**Sir Isaac Newton** (1642–1727)

- **Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy** (The Principia)
    
    - One of the most important works in the history of science. Newton defines the three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation, providing a unified mathematical framework for understanding the movement of objects on Earth and in the heavens.
        
- **Optics**
    
    - A study of the nature of light and color. Newton demonstrates through prism experiments that white light is composed of a spectrum of colors and argues for the "corpuscular" (particle) theory of light.
        

**Christiaan Huygens** (1629–1695)

- **Treatise on Light**
    
    - Written as a counterpoint to Newton, Huygens proposes the "wave theory" of light to explain reflection and refraction.
        

## Volume 35
**John Locke** (1632–1704)

- **A Letter Concerning Toleration**
    
    - A foundational text for liberal democracy, arguing that the state should not enforce religious conformity and that religious belief is a matter of individual conscience (though he excludes atheists and Catholics).
        
- **Concerning Civil Government, Second Essay**
    
    - The blueprint for modern democracy. Locke argues that men have natural rights to "life, liberty, and property." Governments are formed by social contract to protect these rights, and if a government fails to do so, the people have a right to revolt.
        
- **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding**
    
    - A massive work establishing Empiricism. Locke argues against "innate ideas," claiming the mind at birth is a "tabula rasa" (blank slate) and that all knowledge comes from experience (sensation and reflection).
        

**George Berkeley** (1685–1753)

- **The Principles of Human Knowledge**
    
    - An argument for "Idealism" or "Immaterialism." Berkeley contends that material substance does not exist independently of the mind; objects are just collections of ideas. His famous maxim is _esse est percipi_ ("to be is to be perceived").
        

**David Hume** (1711–1776)

- **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding**
    
    - A refinement of empiricism that leads to skepticism. Hume argues that we cannot rationally justify our belief in "cause and effect"—we only see one event following another (constant conjunction) and form a habit of expectation. He also famously attacks the possibility of proving miracles.

## Volume 36
**Jonathan Swift** (1667–1745)

- **Gulliver’s Travels**
    
    - A biting satire of human nature and travel narratives. Lemuel Gulliver visits four strange lands:
        
        - **Lilliput:** Inhabited by tiny people (representing petty politics).
            
        - **Brobdingnag:** Inhabited by giants (magnifying human grossness).
            
        - **Laputa:** A flying island of absent-minded intellectuals (satirizing useless science).
            
        - **Houyhnhnm Land:** Ruled by rational horses who keep savage humans called "Yahoos" as livestock.
            

**Laurence Sterne** (1713–1768)

- **The Life and Opinions of Tristram Shandy, Gent.**
    
    - A wildly experimental comic novel. Tristram attempts to tell his life story but is constantly distracted by digressions, anecdotes, and his eccentric family (like Uncle Toby and his war hobby). It playfully deconstructs the idea of linear storytelling.
        

## Volume 37
**Henry Fielding** (1707–1754)

- **The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling**
    
    - A masterpiece of the picaresque novel. It follows the adventures of the good-natured but imprudent Tom Jones as he travels across England to win the hand of his neighbor Sophia Western, despite his illegitimate birth and numerous amorous mishaps.
        

## Volume 38
**Charles de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu** (1689–1755)

- **The Spirit of Laws**
    
    - A monumental work of political sociology. Montesquieu analyzes how laws differ based on the "spirit" of the people, climate, and geography. Most famously, he articulates the doctrine of the **Separation of Powers** (legislative, executive, judicial) to prevent tyranny, which heavily influenced the U.S. Constitution.
        

**Jean Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778)

- **A Discourse on the Origin of Inequality**
    
    - Argues that man is naturally good ("Noble Savage") but is corrupted by civilization and private property, which introduced inequality and vice.
        
- **A Discourse on Political Economy**
    
    - An encyclopedia entry discussing the concept of the "General Will" in relation to state administration.
        
- **The Social Contract**
    
    - A revolutionary text beginning with "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Rousseau argues that legitimate political authority comes only from a social contract agreed upon by all citizens for their mutual preservation, guided by the "General Will" (the collective good).
        

## Volume 39
**Adam Smith** (1723–1790)

- **An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations**
    
    - The foundational text of modern economics. Smith attacks mercantilism (state-controlled trade) and argues for free markets and free trade. He introduces key concepts like the **Division of Labor**, the **Invisible Hand** (self-interest leading to public benefit), and the function of supply and demand.
        

## Volume 40
**Edward Gibbon** (1737–1794)

- **The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire [Chapters 1–40]**
    
    - The first half of Gibbon's majestic history, covering the period from the height of the Empire (the Antonines) to the fall of the Western Empire. Gibbon is famous for his ironic, elegant prose and his controversial thesis that the rise of Christianity contributed to Rome's fall by weakening its military spirit and civic virtue.

## Volume 41
**Edward Gibbon** (1737–1794)

- **The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire [Chapters 41–71]**
    
    - The second half of Gibbon's history. It covers the history of the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire, the rise of Islam and the conquests of the Caliphate, the Crusades, the Mongol invasions, and finally the capture of Constantinople by the Ottoman Turks in 1453. It concludes with reflections on the medieval ruins of Rome.
        

## Volume 42
**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804)

- **The Critique of Pure Reason**
    
    - A revolutionary work in epistemology. Kant argues that the mind is not a blank slate but actively structures our experience through "categories" (like cause and effect) and forms of intuition (Space and Time). He draws a limit to human knowledge: we can know things as they appear to us (_phenomena_), but not as they are in themselves (_noumena_), thus making room for faith.
        
- **Fundamental Principles of the Metaphysic of Morals**
    
    - The groundwork for Kant’s ethics, introducing the **Categorical Imperative**: "Act only on that maxim whereby thou canst at the same time will that it should become a universal law." He argues that moral actions must be done from duty, not just inclination.
        
- **The Critique of Practical Reason**
    
    - Explores the nature of moral will and freedom. Kant argues that while we cannot scientifically prove God, freedom, or immortality exists, we must "postulate" them as necessary conditions for the moral life.
        
- **The Critique of Judgement**
    
    - Kant’s treatise on aesthetics and teleology. He analyzes the nature of the "Beautiful" (disinterested pleasure) and the "Sublime" (awe-inspiring greatness), and discusses whether there is purpose (teleology) in nature.
        
- **The Science of Right**
    
    - Applied legal and political philosophy, discussing property rights, contract law, and the state.
        

## Volume 43
**American State Papers**

- **The Declaration of Independence** (1776)
    
    - The founding document of the U.S., asserting natural rights ("Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness") and listing grievances against King George III to justify separation from Britain.
        
- **Articles of Confederation** (1777)
    
    - The first constitution of the U.S., creating a weak central government that eventually proved inadequate, leading to the Constitutional Convention.
        
- **The Constitution of the United States of America** (1787)
    
    - The supreme law of the U.S., establishing the three branches of government (Legislative, Executive, Judicial) and the system of checks and balances.
        

**Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, John Jay** (1787–1788)

- **The Federalist** (The Federalist Papers)
    
    - A series of 85 essays arguing for the ratification of the Constitution. They explain the logic behind the new government, discussing dangers like "factions" (Federalist No. 10) and the necessity of checks and balances (Federalist No. 51).
        

**John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873)

- **On Liberty**
    
    - A classic defense of individual freedom against the "tyranny of the majority." Mill articulates the **Harm Principle**: power can only be rightfully exercised over a member of a civilized community against his will to prevent harm to others.
        
- **Representative Government**
    
    - An analysis of how representative democracy works best, advocating for universal suffrage (including women), proportional representation, and an educated citizenry.
        
- **Utilitarianism**
    
    - Defines the ethical theory that actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and wrong as they tend to produce the reverse. Mill distinguishes between higher (intellectual) and lower (sensual) pleasures.
        

## Volume 44
**James Boswell** (1740–1795)

- **The Life of Samuel Johnson, LL.D.**
    
    - Often considered the greatest biography in the English language. Boswell records in minute detail the conversations, wit, habits, and prejudices of the great literary figure Dr. Samuel Johnson. It paints a vivid portrait of literary London in the 18th century.
        

## Volume 45
**Antoine Laurent Lavoisier** (1743–1794)

- **Elements of Chemistry**
    
    - The book that launched the chemical revolution. Lavoisier disproves the old "phlogiston" theory, defines the role of oxygen in combustion, establishes the Law of Conservation of Mass, and creates the modern system of chemical nomenclature (naming elements and compounds).
        

**Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier** (1768–1830)

- **Analytical Theory of Heat**
    
    - A landmark in mathematical physics. Fourier develops the mathematics of heat conduction and introduces "Fourier series," a method of breaking down complex periodic functions into simple waves, which is fundamental to modern signal processing and physics.
        

**Michael Faraday** (1791–1867)

- **Experimental Researches in Electricity**
    
    - A record of the experiments that established the concepts of the electromagnetic field. Faraday discovers electromagnetic induction (how to generate electricity from magnetism), the laws of electrolysis, and the relationship between light and magnetism.

## Volume 46
**Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel** (1770–1831)

- **The Philosophy of Right**
    
    - Hegel’s major work on political philosophy. He analyzes the development of "Objective Spirit" through three stages: Abstract Right (property and contracts), Morality (intentions and guilt), and Ethical Life (_Sittlichkeit_), which culminates in the State—the highest realization of freedom and reason on earth.
        
- **The Philosophy of History**
    
    - Based on his lectures, this work presents history not as a random series of events, but as a rational process—the "World Spirit" becoming conscious of its own freedom. He famously traces this progress from the East (one is free) to the Greeks/Romans (some are free) to the Germanic world (all are free).
        

## Volume 47
**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** (1749–1832)

- **Faust**
    
    - The masterpiece of German literature. It is a dramatic poem in two parts about a scholar, Faust, who is dissatisfied with the limits of human knowledge. He makes a wager with the devil Mephistopheles: if the devil can give him a moment of pure contentment that makes him say "Linger a while, thou art so fair," Mephistopheles can have his soul. It explores themes of striving, redemption, and the demonic.
        

## Volume 48
**Herman Melville** (1819–1882)

- **Moby Dick; or, The Whale**
    
    - An epic American novel narrated by the sailor Ishmael. It tells the story of Captain Ahab’s maniacal, vengeful pursuit of Moby Dick, the Great White Whale that bit off his leg. The book is both a realistic adventure of the whaling industry and a profound metaphysical allegory about good, evil, and man’s struggle against nature and God.
        

## Volume 49
**Charles Darwin** (1809–1882)

- **The Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection**
    
    - The book that fundamentally changed our understanding of life. Darwin presents the theory of evolution by natural selection, arguing that species evolve over time through the preservation of favorable variations in the struggle for existence.
        
- **The Descent of Man and Selection in Relation to Sex**
    
    - Darwin applies his theory explicitly to humans, arguing that mankind shares a common ancestor with the great apes. It also explores "sexual selection" (evolution driven by competition for mates) to explain traits like the peacock’s tail or human racial differences.
        

## Volume 50
**Karl Marx** (1818–1883)

- **Capital** (Das Kapital)
    
    - A massive, systematic critique of the political economy of capitalism. Marx analyzes commodities, money, and labor, arguing that capitalism relies on the exploitation of workers (the proletariat) to generate "surplus value" (profit) for the owners (the bourgeoisie), leading inevitably to crisis and collapse.
        

**Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels** (1820–1895)

- **Manifesto of the Communist Party**
    
    - A short, fiery political pamphlet. It famously begins, "A specter is haunting Europe—the specter of communism," and asserts that "the history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles." It calls for a revolution of the working class to overthrow the bourgeoisie and establish a classless society.
