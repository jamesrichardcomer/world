
1. **Tao Te Ching** — Laozi (c. 400 BCE)
   - Ancient Chinese text teaching the art of living in harmony with the Tao, the natural order underlying all existence.

2. **Analects** — Confucius and disciples (c. 300 BCE)
   - Collection of sayings emphasizing virtue, social harmony, and proper conduct through ritual and familial respect.

3. **The Republic** — Plato (c. 380 BCE)
   - Dialogue exploring justice, the ideal state, and the theory of Forms through Socrates' vision of philosopher-kings.

4. **Symposium** — Plato (c. 370 BCE)
   - Philosophical dialogue examining the nature of love through speeches at a drinking party, culminating in Socrates' account of spiritual ascent.

5. **Apology** — Plato (c. 390 BCE)
   - Socrates' defense speech at his trial, asserting that the unexamined life is not worth living.

6. **The Corpus Aristotelicum** — Aristotle (c. 340 BCE)
   - Comprehensive treatises covering logic, natural science, metaphysics, ethics, and rhetoric that shaped Western thought for millennia.
   
   1. **Categories** — Foundational work classifying types of being and predication.
   2. **On Interpretation** — Analysis of statements, truth, and logical propositions.
   3. **Prior Analytics** — Systematic treatment of syllogistic logic and deductive reasoning.
   4. **Posterior Analytics** — Theory of scientific demonstration and how we acquire knowledge.
   5. **Topics** — Handbook for dialectical argumentation and probable reasoning.
   6. **On Sophistical Refutations** — Catalog of logical fallacies and deceptive arguments.
   7. **Physics** — Investigation of nature, motion, causation, and the principles of change.
   8. **On the Heavens** — Cosmological theory of celestial bodies and the structure of the universe.
   9. **On Generation and Corruption** — Analysis of how substances come into being and pass away.
   10. **Meteorology** — Study of atmospheric phenomena and elemental transformations.
   11. **History of Animals** — Descriptive zoology cataloging animal characteristics and behaviors.
   12. **Parts of Animals** — Teleological explanation of animal anatomy and functional design.
   13. **Movement of Animals** — Mechanical analysis of how animals initiate and control motion.
   14. **Progression of Animals** — Study of different modes of animal locomotion.
   15. **Generation of Animals** — Theory of sexual reproduction and embryological development.
   16. **On the Soul** — Examination of life, consciousness, and the relationship between body and soul.
   17. **Parva Naturalia** — Short treatises on sensation, memory, sleep, and other psychological phenomena.
   18. **Nicomachean Ethics** — Exploration of virtue, happiness, and the good life through practical wisdom.
   19. **Eudemian Ethics** — Earlier ethical treatise examining virtue and human flourishing.
   20. **Magna Moralia** — Summary of Aristotelian ethics possibly compiled by students.
   21. **Rhetoric** — Systematic analysis of persuasion, argumentation, and public speaking.
   22. **Poetics** — Theory of tragedy, plot structure, and the nature of artistic imitation.
   23. **Metaphysics** — Inquiry into being, substance, causation, and first principles.

24. **Letters from a Stoic** — Seneca (c. 65 CE)
   - Practical advice on living virtuously, managing emotions, and facing death with equanimity.

8. **Meditations** — Marcus Aurelius (c. 180 CE)
   - Roman emperor's private reflections on Stoic philosophy, duty, and accepting fate.

9. **Enneads** — Plotinus (c. 270 CE)
   - Neoplatonic philosophy describing reality as emanating from the transcendent One through hierarchical levels.

10. **Confessions** — Augustine of Hippo (c. 400 CE)
    - Autobiographical work exploring sin, conversion, memory, and the nature of time through Christian theology.

11. **City of God** — Augustine of Hippo (426 CE)
    - Defense of Christianity contrasting the earthly city of man with the eternal City of God.

12. **Consolation of Philosophy** — Boethius (c. 524)
    - Dialogue with Lady Philosophy offering Stoic and Platonic comfort during imprisonment before execution.

13. **Mishneh Torah** — Maimonides (c. 1180)
    - Comprehensive codification of Jewish law systematizing the entire Talmud.

14. **Guide for the Perplexed** — Maimonides (c. 1190)
    - Reconciliation of Aristotelian philosophy with Jewish scripture for intellectually troubled believers.

15. **On the Harmony of Religion and Philosophy** — Averroes (Ibn Rushd) (c. 1198)
    - Argument that reason and revelation lead to the same truth, defending philosophy against religious literalism.

16. **Commentaries on Aristotle (Early)** — Thomas Aquinas (c. 1260)
    - Detailed expositions integrating Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology.
    
    1. **Commentary on Aristotle's Metaphysics** — Analysis of being, substance, and God as unmoved mover.
    2. **Commentary on Aristotle's Physics** — Interpretation of natural philosophy and causation.
    3. **Commentary on Aristotle's On the Soul** — Exploration of psychology and the rational soul's immortality.
    4. **Commentary on Aristotle's Posterior Analytics** — Theory of scientific demonstration and knowledge.
    5. **Commentary on Aristotle's On Interpretation** — Logical analysis of propositions and truth.
    6. **Commentary on Aristotle's On the Heavens** — Cosmological interpretation harmonized with Christian doctrine.

17. **Summa Contra Gentiles** — Thomas Aquinas (c. 1265)
    - Rational defense of Christian theology aimed at convincing non-believers through philosophical arguments.

18. **Disputed Questions, Gospel & Ethics Commentaries (Later)** — Thomas Aquinas (c. 1270)
    - Detailed theological investigations and scriptural commentaries from Aquinas's mature period.
    
    1. **Disputed Questions: De Veritate** — Exploration of truth, knowledge, and divine illumination.
    2. **Disputed Questions: De Potentia** — Analysis of God's power and creative activity.
    3. **Disputed Questions: De Malo** — Investigation of evil, sin, and vice.
    4. **Disputed Questions: De Spiritualibus Creaturis** — Treatment of angels and spiritual beings.
    5. **Disputed Questions: De Anima** — Inquiry into the nature and powers of the soul.
    6. **Disputed Questions: De Virtutibus** — Examination of virtues in general.
    7. **Disputed Questions: De Caritate** — Analysis of charity and love.
    8. **Commentary on the Gospels: Matthew** — Theological interpretation of Matthew's Gospel.
    9. **Commentary on the Gospels: John** — Exposition of John's Gospel and its philosophical themes.
    10. **Commentary on Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics** — Christian reading of Aristotelian virtue ethics.

19. **Summa Theologiae** — Thomas Aquinas (c. 1274)
    - Monumental systematic theology synthesizing Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine.

20. **Novum Organum** — Francis Bacon (1620)
    - New method of scientific inquiry emphasizing induction and systematic observation over scholastic deduction.

21. **Essays** — Francis Bacon (1625)
    - Practical wisdom on topics from truth and death to studies and friendship.

22. **Meditations on First Philosophy** — René Descartes (1641)
    - Foundational work establishing "I think, therefore I am" and proving God's existence through systematic doubt.

23. **Ethics** — Baruch Spinoza (1677)
    - Geometric demonstration that God and Nature are one substance, and freedom comes through understanding necessity.

24. **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding** — John Locke (1690)
    - Empiricist account of how all knowledge derives from sensory experience, rejecting innate ideas.

25. **Letters on England** — Voltaire (1733)
    - Satirical comparison praising English tolerance, empiricism, and constitutional government over French absolutism.

26. **A Treatise of Human Nature** — David Hume (1739)
    - Radical empiricism arguing reason is slave to the passions and causation is just habitual expectation.

27. **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding** — David Hume (1748)
    - Accessible reformulation questioning miracles, causation, and the foundations of knowledge.

28. **Candide** — Voltaire (1759)
    - Satirical novella mocking Leibnizian optimism through a young man's catastrophic adventures.

29. **The Theory of Moral Sentiments** — Adam Smith (1759)
    - Account of morality based on sympathy and the impartial spectator judging our sentiments.

30. **Critique of Pure Reason** — Immanuel Kant (1781)
    - Revolutionary analysis of how the mind structures experience, limiting knowledge to save room for faith.

31. **Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals** — Immanuel Kant (1785)
    - Foundation of duty-based ethics grounded in the categorical imperative to treat humanity as an end.

32. **Critique of Practical Reason** — Immanuel Kant (1788)
    - Exploration of moral law, autonomy, and how practical reason postulates God and immortality.

33. **Critique of Judgment** — Immanuel Kant (1790)
    - Analysis of aesthetic judgment, beauty, the sublime, and teleology in nature.

34. **Phenomenology of Spirit** — G.W.F. Hegel (1807)
    - Dialectical journey of consciousness from sense-certainty to absolute knowledge through historical development.

35. **Science of Logic** — G.W.F. Hegel (1812)
    - Systematic exposition of thought's self-development through dialectical contradictions toward the Absolute Idea.

36. **The World as Will and Representation** — Arthur Schopenhauer (1818)
    - Pessimistic metaphysics viewing reality as blind striving will, escapable only through aesthetic contemplation and asceticism.

37. **Either/Or** — Søren Kierkegaard (1843)
    - Pseudonymous exploration of aesthetic versus ethical life-views and the leap of existential choice.

38. **Fear and Trembling** — Søren Kierkegaard (1843)
    - Meditation on Abraham's sacrifice examining faith as absurd subjective relationship transcending ethics.

39. **Thus Spoke Zarathustra** — Friedrich Nietzsche (1883)
    - Poetic proclamation of God's death, eternal recurrence, and the übermensch who creates values beyond good and evil.

40. **Beyond Good and Evil** — Friedrich Nietzsche (1886)
    - Critique of traditional morality as life-denying herd mentality disguising will to power.

41. **On the Genealogy of Morals** — Friedrich Nietzsche (1887)
    - Historical psychology tracing Christian morality to ressentiment of the weak against noble values.

42. **The Varieties of Religious Experience** — William James (1902)
    - Pragmatic study of religious consciousness arguing faith's truth lies in its experiential and practical effects.

43. **Principia Ethica** — G.E. Moore (1903)
    - Foundational work in analytic ethics arguing "good" is indefinable and committing the naturalistic fallacy.

44. **Pragmatism** — William James (1907)
    - Method treating truth as what works practically, validated by consequences rather than correspondence.

45. **Principia Mathematica** — Alfred North Whitehead & Bertrand Russell (1910)
    - Monumental attempt to derive mathematics from logical axioms, foundational to analytic philosophy.

46. **The Problems of Philosophy** — Bertrand Russell (1912)
    - Accessible introduction to epistemology, metaphysics, and the nature of philosophical inquiry.

47. **Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus** — Ludwig Wittgenstein (1921)
    - Terse demonstration that language pictures reality, whereof one cannot speak thereof one must be silent.

48. **Being and Time** — Martin Heidegger (1927)
    - Phenomenological investigation of human existence (Dasein) as being-toward-death revealing Being itself.

49. **The Myth of Sisyphus** — Albert Camus (1942)
    - Absurdist essay arguing we must imagine Sisyphus happy despite life's meaninglessness, rejecting suicide and embracing revolt.

50. **Being and Nothingness** — Jean-Paul Sartre (1943)
    - Existentialist phenomenology asserting existence precedes essence and we're condemned to be free.

51. **The Rebel** — Albert Camus (1951)
    - Examination of metaphysical and historical rebellion, rejecting both nihilism and revolution that creates new tyrannies.

52. **A Theory of Justice** — John Rawls (1971)
    - Liberal political philosophy grounding justice in principles rational agents would choose behind a veil of ignorance.

53. **Discipline and Punish** — Michel Foucault (1975)
    - Genealogy showing how modern power operates through surveillance and normalization rather than sovereign violence.

54. **The History of Sexuality, Vol. 1** — Michel Foucault (1976)
    - Argument that sexuality wasn't repressed but proliferated as object of scientific discourse and power.

55. **Simulacra and Simulation** — Jean Baudrillard (1981)
    - Postmodern analysis claiming contemporary reality is replaced by signs and simulations with no original referent.
