
1. **The Iliad — Homer (c. 8th century BCE)**
   - Epic poem recounting the final year of the Trojan War, centered on Achilles' wrath and the heroic code of ancient Greece.

2. **The Odyssey — Homer (c. 8th century BCE)**
   - Epic tale of Odysseus's ten-year journey home after the Trojan War, facing mythical dangers and divine intervention.

3. **Aesop's Fables — Aesop (traditionally c. 6th century BCE)**
   - Collection of brief moral tales featuring animals that teach practical wisdom through memorable stories.

4. **Epic of Gilgamesh — Unknown (c. 2100 BCE)**
   - Ancient Mesopotamian epic following a king's quest for immortality and his acceptance of human mortality.

5. **Oedipus Rex — Sophocles (c. 429 BCE)**
   - Greek tragedy in which a king unknowingly fulfills a prophecy by killing his father and marrying his mother.

6. **Aeneid — Virgil (29–19 BCE)**
   - Roman epic tracing the hero Aeneas's journey from fallen Troy to Italy, where his descendants will found Rome.

7. **Beowulf — Unknown (c. 8th–10th century)**
   - Old English epic chronicling a Scandinavian warrior's battles against the monster Grendel, Grendel's mother, and a dragon.

8. **The Divine Comedy — Dante Alighieri (c. 1308–1320)**
   - Allegorical journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise, blending Christian theology with classical wisdom.

9. **The Canterbury Tales — Geoffrey Chaucer (c. 1387–1400)**
   - Frame narrative of pilgrims traveling to Canterbury, each telling stories that reveal medieval English society.

10. **Gargantua and Pantagruel — François Rabelais (c. 1532)**
    - Satirical fantasy following two giants whose adventures lampoon Renaissance education, religion, and politics.

11. **Don Quixote — Miguel de Cervantes (Part I 1605; Part II 1615)**
    - Aging gentleman driven mad by chivalric romances sets out as a knight-errant, tilting at windmills in tragicomic adventures.

12. **Essays — Michel de Montaigne (1580–1595)**
    - Personal reflections on human nature, knowledge, and experience that invented the essay as a literary form.

13. **Hamlet — William Shakespeare (c. 1600)**
    - Danish prince feigns madness while wrestling with revenge, mortality, and existential doubt after his father's murder.

14. **Othello — William Shakespeare (c. 1603–1604)**
    - Moorish general's jealousy, manipulated by the villainous Iago, leads to the tragic destruction of his love and life.

15. **King Lear — William Shakespeare (c. 1605–1606)**
    - Aging king's decision to divide his kingdom based on flattery triggers madness, betrayal, and catastrophic loss.

16. **Macbeth — William Shakespeare (c. 1606)**
    - Scottish nobleman's ambition, spurred by prophecy and his wife, drives him to murder and tyrannical madness.

17. **Sonnets — William Shakespeare (1609)**
    - Collection of 154 poems exploring love, beauty, time, mortality, and desire with unmatched linguistic artistry.

18. **The Tempest — William Shakespeare (c. 1610–1611)**
    - Exiled duke uses magic on an enchanted island to orchestrate reconciliation and his daughter's romance.

19. **Paradise Lost — John Milton (1667)**
    - Blank verse epic retelling Satan's rebellion and humanity's fall from Eden, justifying God's ways to mankind.

20. **Pride and Prejudice — Jane Austen (1813)**
    - Sharp-witted Elizabeth Bennet overcomes pride and prejudice to find love with the seemingly aloof Mr. Darcy.

21. **Emma — Jane Austen (1815)**
    - Well-meaning but misguided young woman learns humility through her disastrous matchmaking schemes.

22. **Frankenstein — Mary Shelley (1818)**
    - Scientist creates life from dead matter, only to be haunted by his abandoned, vengeful creation.

23. **Tales and Poems — Edgar Allan Poe (1830s–1840s)**
    - Gothic tales and haunting verse exploring death, madness, and the macabre with psychological intensity.

24. **The Hunchback of Notre-Dame — Victor Hugo (1831)**
    - Deformed bell-ringer's tragic love for a dancer unfolds against medieval Paris's social injustice and religious hypocrisy.

25. **David Copperfield — Charles Dickens (1849–1850)**
    - Semi-autobiographical journey from orphaned boyhood through hardship to success and self-discovery.

26. **The Scarlet Letter — Nathaniel Hawthorne (1850)**
    - Puritan woman bears the shame of adultery while her lover's hidden guilt consumes him from within.

27. **Moby-Dick — Herman Melville (1851)**
    - Captain Ahab's monomaniacal quest for the white whale that maimed him becomes a meditation on obsession and fate.

28. **Uncle Tom's Cabin — Harriet Beecher Stowe (1852)**
    - Anti-slavery novel depicting the brutal realities of enslaved life that galvanized abolitionist sentiment.

29. **Walden — Henry David Thoreau (1854)**
    - Philosophical memoir of two years living simply in the woods, advocating self-reliance and connection to nature.

30. **Madame Bovary — Gustave Flaubert (1856)**
    - Provincial doctor's wife seeks escape from domestic monotony through romantic affairs, leading to ruin.

31. **Great Expectations — Charles Dickens (1860–1861)**
    - Orphan boy rises from poverty through mysterious patronage, learning that wealth cannot replace integrity and love.

32. **Les Misérables — Victor Hugo (1862)**
    - Ex-convict's redemption and pursuit by a relentless lawman unfold against revolutionary France's social upheaval.

33. **War and Peace — Leo Tolstoy (1865–1869)**
    - Sweeping epic interweaving Russian aristocratic families' lives with Napoleon's invasion and the nature of history.

34. **Crime and Punishment — Fyodor Dostoevsky (1866)**
    - Impoverished student murders a pawnbroker to prove a nihilistic theory, then spirals into psychological torment.

35. **The Idiot — Fyodor Dostoevsky (1868–1869)**
    - Epileptic prince whose Christlike innocence and compassion cannot survive in corrupt Russian high society.

36. **Middlemarch — George Eliot (1871)**
    - Interlocking stories of provincial English life explore idealism, marriage, reform, and the constraints on women.

37. **The Adventures of Tom Sawyer — Mark Twain (1876)**
    - Mischievous boy's escapades in a Mississippi River town capture the spirit of American boyhood.

38. **Anna Karenina — Leo Tolstoy (1877)**
    - Aristocrat's passionate affair destroys her life while a parallel story explores family, faith, and meaning.

39. **The Brothers Karamazov — Fyodor Dostoevsky (1880)**
    - Murder of a debauched father ignites a philosophical and spiritual drama among his three sons about faith and morality.

40. **Adventures of Huckleberry Finn — Mark Twain (1884)**
    - Boy and escaped slave raft down the Mississippi, satirizing racism and hypocrisy in pre-Civil War America.

41. **The Picture of Dorian Gray — Oscar Wilde (1890)**
    - Beautiful young man remains ageless while his hidden portrait bears the marks of his moral corruption.

42. **The Importance of Being Earnest — Oscar Wilde (1895)**
    - Witty comedy of manners satirizing Victorian society through elaborate deceptions about fictional alter egos.

43. **In Search of Lost Time (Remembrance of Things Past) — Marcel Proust (1913–1927)**
    - Monumental exploration of memory, time, art, and society through the narrator's involuntary recollections.

44. **Dubliners — James Joyce (1914)**
    - Fifteen stories depicting the lives of middle-class Dubliners, revealing moments of epiphany and spiritual paralysis.

45. **The Metamorphosis — Franz Kafka (1915)**
    - Salesman wakes transformed into an insect, exploring alienation and the absurdity of modern existence.

46. **A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man — James Joyce (1916)**
    - Semi-autobiographical account of a young Irishman's intellectual and artistic awakening and rejection of conformity.

47. **The Waste Land — T.S. Eliot (1922)**
    - Modernist poem depicting post-WWI spiritual desolation through fragmented voices, myths, and literary allusions.

48. **Ulysses — James Joyce (1922)**
    - Single day in Dublin reimagines Homer's Odyssey through stream-of-consciousness narrative and linguistic experimentation.

49. **Siddhartha — Hermann Hesse (1922)**
    - Young man's spiritual quest in ancient India leads him through worldly experience to enlightenment.

50. **The Trial — Franz Kafka (written 1914–1915; published 1925)**
    - Bank clerk arrested for an unspecified crime navigates a nightmarish, incomprehensible legal system.

51. **The Great Gatsby — F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925)**
    - Mysterious millionaire's obsessive pursuit of his lost love exposes the corruption beneath the Jazz Age's glamour.

52. **The Sun Also Rises — Ernest Hemingway (1926)**
    - Disillusioned expatriates drift through post-WWI Europe, seeking meaning amid lost ideals and emotional wounds.

53. **A Farewell to Arms — Ernest Hemingway (1929)**
    - American ambulance driver's doomed romance with a British nurse unfolds against WWI's Italian front.

54. **The Sound and the Fury — William Faulkner (1929)**
    - Decline of a Southern aristocratic family told through fragmented, stream-of-consciousness perspectives.

55. **As I Lay Dying — William Faulkner (1930)**
    - Poor Southern family's tragicomic journey to bury their mother, narrated by fifteen different characters.

56. **Brave New World — Aldous Huxley (1932)**
    - Dystopian future where technological control, genetic engineering, and pleasure eliminate freedom and individuality.

57. **Of Mice and Men — John Steinbeck (1937)**
    - Two migrant workers dream of owning land during the Depression, but tragedy destroys their fragile hope.

58. **The Hobbit — J.R.R. Tolkien (1937)**
    - Reluctant hobbit joins dwarves on a quest to reclaim their treasure from a dragon, discovering courage along the way.

59. **The Grapes of Wrath — John Steinbeck (1939)**
    - Oklahoma family's migration to California during the Dust Bowl exposes the exploitation and resilience of the dispossessed.

60. **The Stranger — Albert Camus (1942)**
    - Emotionally detached man commits a senseless murder, exploring existentialism and the absurdity of life.

61. **Four Quartets — T.S. Eliot (1943)**
    - Meditative poems on time, memory, faith, and redemption, seeking spiritual meaning in a fragmented world.

62. **Animal Farm — George Orwell (1945)**
    - Barnyard animals overthrow their farmer only to replicate tyranny, satirizing the corruption of revolutionary ideals.

63. **Nineteen Eighty-Four — George Orwell (1949)**
    - Totalitarian state uses surveillance, propaganda, and torture to crush individuality and control truth itself.

64. **The Catcher in the Rye — J.D. Salinger (1951)**
    - Alienated teenager wanders New York City, railing against the "phoniness" of adult society.

65. **Waiting for Godot — Samuel Beckett (1952/1953)**
    - Two men wait endlessly for someone who never comes, in an absurdist meditation on meaning and existence.

66. **The Lord of the Rings — J.R.R. Tolkien (1954–1955)**
    - Epic fantasy quest to destroy a powerful ring and defeat ultimate evil, exploring courage, friendship, and sacrifice.

67. **Lolita — Vladimir Nabokov (1955)**
    - Unreliable narrator chronicles his obsession with a young girl in a disturbing exploration of manipulation and delusion.

68. **The Chronicles of Narnia — C.S. Lewis (1950–1956)**
    - Seven fantasy novels in which children enter a magical world filled with Christian allegory and adventure.

69. **To Kill a Mockingbird — Harper Lee (1960)**
    - Lawyer defends a Black man falsely accused of rape in Depression-era Alabama, seen through his daughter's eyes.

70. **Catch-22 — Joseph Heller (1961)**
    - Bombardier tries to maintain sanity in WWII, trapped by absurd military bureaucracy and circular logic.

71. **One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich — Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn (1962)**
    - Single day in a Soviet labor camp reveals the brutal reality of Stalin's gulag system.

72. **Dune — Frank Herbert (1965)**
    - Political intrigue and ecological themes converge on a desert planet where a young nobleman becomes a messianic figure.

73. **One Hundred Years of Solitude — Gabriel García Márquez (1967)**
    - Magical realist saga tracing seven generations of a family in a Colombian town doomed to repeat its history.

74. **Slaughterhouse-Five — Kurt Vonnegut (1969)**
    - WWII soldier becomes "unstuck in time," experiencing the Dresden bombing and alien abduction in fragmented anti-war narrative.

75. **The Handmaid's Tale — Margaret Atwood (1985)**
    - Totalitarian theocracy enslaves fertile women as breeders in a dystopian exploration of misogyny and resistance.
