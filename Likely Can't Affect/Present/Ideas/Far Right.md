**Definition:** Politics further right than standard conservatism, distinguished by extremism, exclusionism, and rejection of liberal democratic norms.

### Core Ideological Pillars (Mudde Framework)
1. **Nativism/Ethnonationalism**
   - Belief nation should be exclusively inhabited by homogeneous "native" group
   - Defined by race, ethnicity, religion, culture, or "blood" (not citizenship/law)
   - Opposition to immigration, multiculturalism, ethnic mixing
   - "Great Replacement" conspiracy theory (white population being deliberately replaced)

2. **Authoritarianism**
   - Strong preference for hierarchical social order and obedience to authority
   - Anti-democratic or anti-liberal attitudes
   - Support for strong leaders with cult of personality
   - Rejection of minority rights, rule of law, separation of powers
   - Extreme forms reject democracy entirely (not just liberal aspects)

3. **Populism**
   - "The people" vs. "corrupt elites" and establishment
   - Anti-party system, anti-institutional
   - Often combined with nationalism to exclude outgroups

### Key Characteristics
**Exclusivism:**
- Racism, xenophobia, ethnocentrism
- Belief in natural hierarchies (superior vs. inferior groups)
- Social Darwinism (superior people deserve greater rights)

**Traditionalism:**
- Lament disappearance of historic frames (traditional family, ethnic/religious community)
- Opposition to gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights ("gender ideology")
- Law and order rhetoric

**Anti-Egalitarianism:**
- Rejection that inequalities should be corrected
- Upholds power privileges of certain elites over society
- Hierarchical worldview with dominance/subjugation

**Conspiracy Theories:**
- Antisemitic narratives, globalist conspiracies
- Cultural Marxism, deep state theories
- Civilization under siege narratives

### Historical Forms
- **Classical:** Fascism (Italy), Nazism (Germany), reactionary conservatism
- **Contemporary:** Neo-fascism, neo-Nazism, white nationalism, alt-right, Third Position
- **Regional variants:** Hindu nationalism (India), Christian nationalism (US/Europe)

### Contemporary Manifestations (2025)
**Ideological Variants:**
- **Ethnic Nationalism:** Preserving ethnic unity, opposing immigration (less extreme)
- **Racial Nationalism:** White supremacy, race war, neo-Nazi ideology (most extreme)
- **Accelerationism:** Using violence to destabilize system (Siege culture, Atomwaffen Division)

**Methods:**
- Electoral politics (AfD Germany, National Rally France, others)
- Extra-parliamentary movements, street activism
- Online radicalization networks and influencers
- Terrorism and political violence (extreme fringe)

### Psychological Profile
Research shows adherents often exhibit:
- Need for cognitive closure, order, structure, certainty
- Rigid worldview, simplified black-and-white thinking
- Desire for group-based dominance and hierarchy
- Authoritarianism and traditionalism
- Lower tolerance for ambiguity and uncertainty

### Distinction from Mainstream Conservatism
**Far Right differs by:**
- Rejecting liberal democracy itself (not just disagreeing on policy)
- Embracing ethnic/racial exclusion (vs. civic nationalism)
- Accepting/promoting political violence
- Totalitarian or authoritarian aims
- Conspiracy-driven worldview

**Mainstream conservatism** operates within democratic norms, accepts pluralism, and uses democratic processes.

### Global Presence (2025)
Growing influence in: Europe (Hungary, Italy, Poland), Americas (various), India (BJP/RSS), rise in far-right terrorism globally (especially white supremacist attacks)

### Note
The term "far right" is analytical; those labeled as such typically reject the designation, preferring "nationalist" or "patriot."
