## How the Brain Works (basics)
The brain has ~86 billion neurons connected by ~100 trillion synapses. Thoughts, feelings, and behaviors emerge from electrochemical signals between neurons.

**Key structures:**
- **Prefrontal cortex** — Planning, decision-making, impulse control. Last part to fully develop (~age 25).
- **Amygdala** — Threat detection, fear, emotional reactions. Responds faster than conscious thought.
- **Hippocampus** — Memory formation and spatial navigation.
- **Basal ganglia** — Habit formation, routine behaviors.
- **Brain stem** — Breathing, heart rate, sleep — automatic survival functions.

**Two systems of thinking (Kahneman):**
- **System 1** — Fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Default mode. Most daily decisions.
- **System 2** — Slow, deliberate, effortful, logical. Activated for complex problems. Tires easily.

Most of what we do is System 1. We think we're being rational (System 2) far more often than we actually are.

## Cognitive Biases (how thinking goes wrong)
**Biases about ourselves:**
- **Confirmation bias** — Seeking/favoring information that confirms existing beliefs
- **Self-serving bias** — Attributing success to yourself, failure to circumstances
- **Dunning-Kruger effect** — Low-skill people overestimate ability; high-skill people underestimate it
- **Optimism bias** — Overestimating likelihood of good outcomes for yourself
- **Illusion of control** — Believing you can influence events you actually can't

**Biases about others:**
- **Fundamental attribution error** — Attributing others' behavior to character rather than circumstances ("he's lazy" vs. "he's exhausted")
- **In-group bias** — Favoring people in your own group
- **Halo effect** — Assuming someone good in one way is good in all ways (attractive = competent)
- **Stereotyping** — Applying group-level generalizations to individuals

**Biases in judgment:**
- **Anchoring** — Over-relying on the first piece of information received
- **Availability heuristic** — Judging probability by how easily examples come to mind (plane crashes feel more common than they are)
- **Sunk cost fallacy** — Continuing something because of past investment rather than future value
- **Status quo bias** — Preferring things to stay the same
- **Loss aversion** — Losses feel ~2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good
- **Framing effect** — Decisions change based on how options are presented ("90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate")
- **Recency bias** — Overweighting recent events

## Motivation
**Maslow's Hierarchy (simplified):**
1. Physiological needs (food, water, sleep, shelter)
2. Safety (security, stability, health)
3. Belonging (love, friendship, community)
4. Esteem (respect, status, accomplishment)
5. Self-actualization (reaching your potential, meaning)

Lower needs generally must be met before higher ones become motivating. Not a rigid ladder — people can pursue multiple levels at once.

**Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic motivation:**
- Intrinsic — Doing something because it's inherently satisfying (curiosity, mastery, autonomy)
- Extrinsic — Doing something for external reward/punishment (money, grades, approval)
- Over-rewarding intrinsically motivated behavior can actually undermine it (overjustification effect)

**Self-determination theory** — People need three things to thrive: autonomy (control), competence (mastery), relatedness (connection).

## Emotions
**Basic emotions (Ekman):** happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust — recognizable across all cultures.

**Emotions vs. feelings vs. moods:**
- Emotion — Automatic physiological response (milliseconds)
- Feeling — Conscious experience of an emotion (seconds to minutes)
- Mood — Prolonged emotional state (hours to days), often without clear cause

**Emotional regulation** — The ability to manage emotional responses. Develops through childhood; requires prefrontal cortex. Strategies: reappraisal (reframing the situation), suppression (hiding the response), distraction, acceptance.

## Social Psychology (how others affect us)
- **Conformity** — Adjusting behavior to match the group (Asch experiments: people gave obviously wrong answers to match the group)
- **Obedience** — Following authority even when it conflicts with conscience (Milgram experiments: most people administered what they believed were dangerous shocks when instructed)
- **Bystander effect** — Less likely to help when others are present
- **Social proof** — Using others' behavior as guide for your own
- **Groupthink** — Group desire for harmony overrides realistic evaluation of alternatives
- **Tribalism** — Strong tendency to form in-groups and out-groups; evolved for survival in small bands

## Personality
**Big Five (OCEAN):**

| Trait | High | Low |
|---|---|---|
| Openness | Creative, curious, adventurous | Practical, conventional, routine |
| Conscientiousness | Organized, disciplined, reliable | Flexible, spontaneous, careless |
| Extraversion | Outgoing, energetic, talkative | Quiet, reserved, solitary |
| Agreeableness | Cooperative, trusting, helpful | Competitive, skeptical, challenging |
| Neuroticism | Anxious, moody, emotionally reactive | Calm, stable, resilient |

These are relatively stable across adulthood. ~50% heritable, ~50% environment.

## Development
- **Critical periods** — Windows where the brain is especially receptive to certain learning (language, attachment)
- **Attachment theory** — Early bond with caregiver shapes relationship patterns for life. Secure attachment → healthier relationships.
- **Prefrontal cortex** doesn't fully develop until ~25 — teenagers are more impulsive, sensation-seeking, and peer-influenced not from poor character but from brain development.

## Evolutionary Psychology (why we're wired this way)
Human psychology evolved for survival in small hunter-gatherer bands (~150 people) on the African savanna. Many modern problems come from this mismatch:

- **Fear of rejection** — Exile from the group meant death; explains social anxiety
- **Tribalism** — Needed to distinguish friend from foe quickly
- **Status-seeking** — Higher status = better survival and reproduction
- **Loss aversion** — Losing resources was more dangerous than failing to gain new ones
- **Preference for calorie-dense food** — Scarce on the savanna; now abundant → obesity
- **Short-term thinking** — Immediate threats mattered more than long-term planning
- **Gossip** — Social monitoring tool for groups; keeps cheaters in check

## Mental Health (prevalence in US)
- ~1 in 5 adults experience mental illness in a given year
- Depression: ~8% of adults
- Anxiety disorders: ~19% of adults
- PTSD: ~3.5% of adults
- Bipolar disorder: ~2.8% of adults
- Schizophrenia: ~1% of adults
- Suicide is a leading cause of death for ages 10–34
