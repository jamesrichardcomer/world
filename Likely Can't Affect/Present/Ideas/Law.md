## Legal Systems of the World
| System | Based On | Where |
|---|---|---|
| Common law | Judicial precedent (case law) + statutes | US, UK, Canada, Australia, India, most former British colonies |
| Civil law | Codified statutes and legal codes | Continental Europe, Latin America, much of Asia and Africa |
| Religious law | Sacred texts and religious doctrine | Parts of Middle East (Sharia), Israel (personal matters), Vatican |
| Customary law | Traditions and community practices | Parts of Africa, indigenous communities |
| Mixed | Combination of the above | Many countries blend systems (e.g., Louisiana, Quebec, South Africa, Japan) |

~150 countries use civil law. ~80 use common law or mixed systems.

## The US Legal System
### Structure
**Federal system** — Two parallel court systems: federal and state. Each state has its own constitution, statutes, and courts.

**Federal courts (3 levels):**
1. District Courts (94) — Trial courts; where cases start
2. Circuit Courts of Appeals (13) — Review district court decisions
3. Supreme Court (1, 9 justices) — Final authority on constitutional questions; hears ~70 cases/year

**State courts (each state has its own hierarchy):**
1. Trial courts (district/county courts)
2. Appellate courts
3. State Supreme Court

**Which court?** Federal courts handle: federal law, constitutional questions, disputes between states, cases involving the US government. State courts handle everything else (most criminal cases, family law, property, contracts, torts).

### Sources of Law (hierarchy)
1. **US Constitution** — Supreme law of the land
2. **Federal statutes** — Laws passed by Congress
3. **Federal regulations** — Rules created by executive agencies (EPA, SEC, FDA, etc.)
4. **State constitutions**
5. **State statutes**
6. **State regulations**
7. **Case law / precedent** — Judicial decisions that interpret all of the above

**Stare decisis** — Courts follow precedent set by higher courts in their jurisdiction. This is what makes common law systems distinctive.

### Key Areas of Law
**Criminal law** — Government prosecutes individuals for offenses against society. Burden of proof: beyond a reasonable doubt. Penalties: fines, imprisonment, probation, death (in some states).

**Civil law (private)** — Disputes between individuals/organizations. Burden of proof: preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not). Remedies: money damages, injunctions, specific performance.

**Constitutional law** — Interpretation of the Constitution. Bill of Rights, due process, equal protection, separation of powers, federalism.

**Contract law** — Enforceable agreements. Elements: offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, legality.

**Tort law** — Civil wrongs causing harm. Negligence (failure to exercise reasonable care), intentional torts (assault, fraud), strict liability (liability without fault, e.g., defective products).

**Property law** — Real property (land/buildings) and personal property. Ownership, transfers, leases, easements, zoning.

**Family law** — Marriage, divorce, child custody, adoption, domestic violence.

**Administrative law** — Governs how government agencies make and enforce regulations. Agencies have quasi-legislative (rulemaking) and quasi-judicial (enforcement/hearings) powers.

**Corporate/Business law** — Formation, governance, and dissolution of business entities. Mergers, securities, antitrust.

## International Law
**Public international law** — Governs relations between states. Sources: treaties, customary international law, general principles, UN resolutions.

**Private international law (conflict of laws)** — Determines which country's law applies in cross-border disputes.

**Key institutions:**
- International Court of Justice (ICJ) — UN's principal judicial body; settles disputes between states
- International Criminal Court (ICC) — Prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity. 124 member states; US, Russia, China, India are not members.
- World Trade Organization (WTO) — Dispute resolution for trade conflicts

**Limitations:** No world government to enforce international law. Compliance depends largely on voluntary cooperation, diplomatic pressure, and sanctions. Powerful states frequently ignore international law without consequence.

## How a Lawsuit Works (US Civil, simplified)
1. **Pre-suit** — Dispute arises; demand letter; negotiation
2. **Filing** — Plaintiff files complaint; defendant is served
3. **Answer** — Defendant responds; may file counterclaims or motions to dismiss
4. **Discovery** — Both sides exchange evidence (documents, depositions, interrogatories)
5. **Motions** — Summary judgment, motions in limine (to exclude evidence)
6. **Trial** — Jury or bench trial; opening statements, evidence, cross-examination, closing arguments, verdict
7. **Post-trial** — Motions for new trial; appeals

~95% of civil cases settle before trial. ~90%+ of criminal cases end in plea bargains.

## Constitutional Amendments (selected)
- **1st** — Free speech, religion, press, assembly, petition
- **2nd** — Right to bear arms
- **4th** — No unreasonable searches/seizures
- **5th** — Due process, no self-incrimination, double jeopardy, eminent domain
- **6th** — Right to speedy trial, counsel, jury, confrontation of witnesses
- **8th** — No cruel and unusual punishment
- **13th** — Abolished slavery
- **14th** — Equal protection, due process (applied to states), citizenship
- **15th** — Right to vote regardless of race
- **19th** — Right to vote regardless of sex

## Key Facts
- ~1.3 million lawyers in the US
- ~40 million lawsuits filed per year in the US
- US incarcerates more people than any other country (~1.9 million; ~0.6% of population)
- Federal judges serve lifetime appointments; state judges vary (elected, appointed, or merit selection)
