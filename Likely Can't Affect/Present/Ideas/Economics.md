## How Markets Work
**Supply and Demand** — Prices are determined by the interaction of buyers (demand) and sellers (supply). When demand exceeds supply, prices rise. When supply exceeds demand, prices fall.

**Markets** — Any arrangement where buyers and sellers exchange goods, services, or assets. Can be physical (farmers market) or abstract (stock exchange, currency markets).

**Profit motive** — Businesses produce what people are willing to pay for. Prices signal where resources should flow. High prices attract new producers; low prices drive them out.

**Competition** — Multiple sellers competing drives prices down and quality up. Monopolies (one seller) and oligopolies (few sellers) reduce this pressure.

## Economic Systems
| System | Key Feature | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Free market capitalism | Private ownership, minimal government | Theoretical ideal; no pure examples |
| Mixed economy | Private markets + government regulation/welfare | US, UK, most of Europe, Japan |
| Social democracy | Capitalist economy + strong welfare state | Scandinavia, Germany |
| State capitalism | Government owns/controls key industries, markets exist | China, Singapore, Saudi Arabia |
| Command economy | Government plans production and distribution | North Korea, Cuba (partially) |

Most real economies are mixed. The debate is about how much government involvement is optimal.

## Money & Banking
**Fiat currency** — Money not backed by a physical commodity (gold). Value comes from government decree and trust. All major currencies today are fiat.

**Central banks** — Control the money supply and set interest rates.
- US: Federal Reserve (the Fed)
- EU: European Central Bank (ECB)
- The Fed's dual mandate: maximum employment + stable prices (target ~2% inflation)

**Interest rates** — The price of borrowing money. When the Fed raises rates, borrowing becomes more expensive, spending slows, and inflation tends to fall (but unemployment may rise). When it lowers rates, the reverse happens.

**Inflation** — General increase in prices over time. Caused by too much money chasing too few goods, supply shocks, or expectations. US inflation peaked ~9% in mid-2022, came down to ~2.5–3% by late 2025.

**Banks** — Take deposits, make loans. They create money through lending (fractional reserve banking). Only keep a fraction of deposits on hand.

## Fiscal Policy (Government)
**Taxation** — How governments raise revenue. Income tax, corporate tax, sales tax, property tax, capital gains tax.

**Government spending** — Where tax revenue (and borrowed money) goes. See your "Government Spending.md" for US breakdown.

**Deficit** — When government spends more than it collects in taxes in a given year.

**National debt** — Accumulated deficits over time. US national debt: ~$36 trillion (early 2026). ~120% of GDP.

**Stimulus vs. Austerity** — Keynesian view: government should spend more during recessions and less during booms. Austerity view: government should cut spending to reduce debt.

## International Trade
**Comparative advantage** — Countries benefit from specializing in what they produce most efficiently and trading for the rest, even if one country is better at everything.

**Tariffs** — Taxes on imports. Protect domestic industries but raise prices for consumers.

**Trade deficit** — When a country imports more than it exports (US has large trade deficit, especially with China).

**Currency exchange rates** — Floating (market-determined) vs. managed/pegged. A weaker currency makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive.

**Global supply chains** — Products assembled from components made in many countries. A single iPhone involves parts from 40+ countries.

## Schools of Economic Thought (simplified)
- **Classical/Neoclassical** — Markets self-correct; minimal government intervention
- **Keynesian** — Government spending needed to stabilize demand during downturns
- **Monetarist** — Control money supply to control inflation (Milton Friedman)
- **Austrian** — Skeptical of central banking and government intervention; emphasizes individual action
- **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT)** — Governments that issue their own currency can't "run out of money"; constraint is inflation, not debt
- **Marxist** — Capitalism exploits labor; means of production should be collectively owned
